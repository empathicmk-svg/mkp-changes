The order of operations isn't arbitrary.

Positioning is the decision about what you are, who you're for, and why you're the right choice for them. Copy is the language that expresses that decision.

If you write copy before you've made the positioning decision — which most people do — the copy is making the positioning decision for you. Usually badly, and usually by defaulting to the safest possible version of the thing.

## What happens when you reverse the order

You write the homepage. The homepage uses broad language because you haven't decided who specifically you're for. The broad language attracts broad traffic. The broad traffic converts at a mediocre rate. You optimize the copy to improve the rate without improving the conversion, because the problem is the audience, not the words.

This is the cycle most companies are in.

It's not a copy problem. It is a sequence problem.

## What positioning actually requires

Positioning requires three decisions, in order:

**The customer decision.** Not a persona — a real decision about which version of your potential market you're most valuable to, and which version you're turning down.

**The problem decision.** Not the category problem — the specific version of the problem that you solve better than anyone else.

**The differentiation decision.** Not what makes you different — what makes you the right choice for the specific customer with the specific problem. The difference between the two is the difference between a feature list and a position.

Until these three decisions are made, copy is guesswork.

## The practical implication

The next time you feel like you need better copy, ask whether the positioning decisions have been made. If they haven't, start there. The copy will take a fraction of the time once the decisions are clear.

The sequence is: positioning, then copy. The copy is only as good as the thinking it expresses.

---

*This is the reason some copy engagements go very fast and some take months. The fast ones are with companies that have made the decisions. The slow ones are making the decisions through the copy, which is the hard way.*
