One is for you. One is for your prospect. You've probably been writing the wrong one.

A mission statement is about what you're trying to achieve. It is aspirational, directional, internal. It tells your team what you're building toward. It can be inspiring. It is almost never useful to a prospect who landed on your homepage at 2pm on a Tuesday with a specific problem.

Positioning is about what you actually are to a specific person at the moment they find you. It is not aspirational. It is descriptive. It answers the question: for this person, with this problem, why is this the right place to be?

## The failure mode

The failure mode is writing your mission statement in the register of positioning, and calling it messaging.

You'll recognize it. It sounds like: *"We believe every team deserves the tools to do their best work."*

That sentence is not false. It is not specific. It tells the prospect nothing about whether they are in the right place. It communicates that you have a belief about teams, which is nice, and gets nobody to the next page.

The prospect has a specific problem. They need to know if you solve it. "We believe teams deserve better tools" does not tell them that.

## What positioning actually answers

Positioning answers five questions, in order:

1. Who is this for?
2. What problem do they have?
3. What is the specific outcome they want?
4. Why is this different from the other options?
5. Why should they believe it?

A mission statement answers none of these. That's fine — it's not supposed to. The mistake is using it where positioning is needed.

## The test

Read your homepage headline. Then answer this: does a stranger with your target customer's problem know, within ten seconds of reading, whether they're in the right place?

If the answer is no, you have a mission statement where positioning should be.

The fix is not creative. It's sequential. Answer the five questions. Write the positioning. Then, if you want, put the mission statement somewhere your team can find it.
