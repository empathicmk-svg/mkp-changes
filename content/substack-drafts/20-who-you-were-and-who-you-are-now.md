The gap between them is not the problem. Pretending the gap doesn't exist is.

There's a version of professional reinvention that looks like a story. Before and after. Old chapter and new chapter. The person you were in your previous industry, and the person you've become.

The story is real. The problem is that most people haven't told it yet — not clearly, not publicly, and often not to themselves.

So they present a muddled version. The LinkedIn that still leads with the old title. The bio that covers twenty years of diverse experience without landing on a specific claim. The pitch that tries to use the old credentials to justify the new work, which requires the listener to do interpretive work that most people won't do.

The gap isn't the liability. The muddle is.

## What the gap actually contains

In the gap between who you were and who you are now is the most interesting thing about you.

The decisions you made. The thing you learned in the previous chapter that made you see the new one differently. The reason the transition wasn't arbitrary — the logic that connects the two versions of the career into one coherent story.

That logic is what makes you legible to the right people. And it's usually the thing that doesn't appear anywhere in the materials.

## How to tell it

Start with the transition itself, not the origin. "After fifteen years building sales teams, I started noticing a pattern in the companies whose numbers moved and the ones that didn't. The difference was almost always upstream of sales." 

That's a beginning. It implies a before, it states a discovery, and it creates a question the reader wants answered.

The reader who follows you from there has already met the version of you that exists now. Not the version that existed fifteen years ago.

## The invitation

Write one paragraph that begins: "After [what came before], I started noticing [what you saw]."

Don't make it a career summary. Make it a discovery.
