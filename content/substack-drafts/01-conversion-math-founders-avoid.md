There's a number sitting in your funnel right now. You've decided not to look at it.

Here's the calculation founders avoid:

Take your monthly site visitors. Multiply by your current conversion rate. Now change that conversion rate by 0.3 percentage points. One-point-two percent becomes one-point-five. Run it forward twelve months. Multiply by your average contract value.

That number is what your current copy is costing you.

Most founders see the result and call it hypothetical. It isn't. It's the compounding cost of language that doesn't close.

## Why founders avoid this math

It's not financial. It's psychological.

If you run the calculation and the answer is $400,000 annually, you have to reckon with how long you've been leaving it there. That's uncomfortable. So instead you frame copy as a qualitative question — "We need to sound better" — and treat it as a nice-to-have. You push it to Q3. Then Q4. Then next year.

The math doesn't wait.

## What the math actually tells you

Copy is not creative output. Copy is a multiplier that sits underneath every other marketing dollar you spend.

Every ad you run points somewhere. That somewhere converts at a rate. The rate is determined, in large part, by whether the words on the page match what the visitor already believes is true about their problem.

If they do, they move. If they don't, you pay for a click that goes nowhere.

Bad copy raises CAC. It lengthens sales cycles. It caps your pricing because prospects can't see the differentiation they'd pay a premium for. It's not a brand issue. It's a revenue issue.

## Run your number

Monthly visitors × current conversion rate = current monthly conversions
Monthly visitors × (current rate + 0.3%) = improved monthly conversions
Difference × 12 × average contract value = annual revenue delta

That's your number. Don't round it. Don't average it. Don't call it optimistic.

Call it the cost of the copy you haven't fixed yet.
