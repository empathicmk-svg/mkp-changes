It's not about you. That's the problem.

Most About pages are written as biography. They tell the story of the company — when it was founded, why the founder got into the space, what the team believes in. This is fine as biography. It is useless as conversion copy.

The prospect landing on your About page is not looking for your story. They are looking for evidence that you are the right choice for their problem. These are different missions. One of them matters to the conversion.

## What the About page is actually for

The About page is where prospects go when they're interested but not yet certain. They've seen the homepage. Something resonated. Now they want to know: can I trust this?

That question is not answered by the founding year or the size of the team. It is answered by two things:

1. Does this person/company understand the problem the way I understand it?
2. Do they have the evidence that they can solve it?

Point of view and proof. That's what the About page should deliver.

## The structure that works

Open with the problem you solve and why you solve it the way you do. Not your history — your position. What do you believe about the problem that led you to build this the way you built it?

Then give the evidence. Not a feature list — the outcomes. What happened for specific clients? What changed?

Then give the human story. This can be short. It earns trust in a different register — it says there's a real person here with a real point of view, not a brand persona generated by committee.

## The test

Read your About page and ask: if I had arrived here with a specific problem, would this page make me more or less certain that I'd found the right place?

If the answer is less, the page is working against you.

---

*The About page is almost always the second or third page in an evaluation flow. Getting it right shortens the "is this real?" stage significantly.*
