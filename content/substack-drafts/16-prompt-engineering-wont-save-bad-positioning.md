The output is only as good as the input. The input is your thinking.

There is a version of the AI-for-marketing conversation that is entirely about prompts. The right prompt structure. The right context window. The right persona instruction. Ask it to be a CMO with twenty years of B2B SaaS experience and watch the copy improve.

It doesn't work that way. Or rather: it improves the style. It doesn't fix the positioning.

## What prompts can and can't do

A better prompt produces a more polished version of the input you gave it. If the input is vague positioning — "we help teams collaborate more effectively" — the output is polished vague positioning.

The polishing can be misleading. The copy reads better. The sentences are tighter. The tone is more confident. So you think the positioning improved when what actually improved is the prose.

These are different. Prose is the vehicle. Positioning is the destination. Better prose pointing at the wrong destination still gets you to the wrong destination, just more confidently.

## The thing that actually fixes positioning

Positioning is fixed by doing the thinking. By answering the uncomfortable questions.

Who specifically is this for? Not a persona name — a real description that you could use to pick them out of a crowd.

What specifically do they believe about their problem before they find you? Not what you wish they believed. What they actually believe.

What specifically are you disagreeing with about how the category solves this problem? Not in a corporate-diplomatic way. In the way you'd say it to a peer.

These answers cannot be prompted out of AI because they require your genuine view. AI doesn't have your genuine view. It has the average of everyone else's.

## The sequence

Do the thinking first. Write the positioning yourself, badly if necessary. Then use AI to improve the prose.

That sequence produces good copy. The reverse produces polished confusion.
