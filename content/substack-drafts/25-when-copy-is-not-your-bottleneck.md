Sometimes I tell people they don't need me. Here's how to know if you're one of them.

Copy is not always the problem. I work in copy and messaging, which means there's a version of every client conversation that ends with me as the solution. That's not always honest.

There are situations where the problem is upstream of copy. When that's the case, fixing the copy is the wrong fix. It costs time and money and produces a measurably better version of the wrong thing.

## When copy is not the bottleneck

**When the traffic isn't targeted.** If your site gets 50,000 visitors a month and 40,000 of them are outside your ICP, a conversion rate problem is a traffic problem, not a copy problem. Better copy on the wrong audience doesn't convert better.

**When the product-market fit is still being validated.** If you don't have a clear, repeatable signal that customers want the specific thing you're selling, copy sharpening is premature. You need more learning, not better language around an uncertain premise.

**When the sales team is the bottleneck.** If your copy is generating qualified leads and the leads are stalling in the sales process, you have a sales problem. More conversion-optimized copy generates more leads who stall.

**When the offer is wrong.** If the pricing, the packaging, or the terms are the actual friction point in the buyer's decision, the headline doesn't fix it. The offer fixes it.

## How to diagnose your actual bottleneck

Map where qualified prospects are dropping out of your funnel. If it's before the homepage, you have a traffic/channel problem. If it's on the page but before the CTA, you might have a copy problem. If it's after the CTA, you probably have a sales or offer problem.

Fix the right thing.

## What I actually do in this situation

When a client describes a problem that sounds like a copy problem and the diagnosis says otherwise, I say so. And then I tell them what kind of help they actually need, even if it's not the kind I provide.

That's what trust is for.
