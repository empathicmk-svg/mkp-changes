Every brand that breaks through has something it disagrees with. Publicly.

Not disagrees with in a hedge-everything, "it depends on your situation" way. Disagrees with in a way that makes some people uncomfortable and others feel like they've finally heard someone say the thing.

This is not a marketing tactic. It is how positioning works. You cannot occupy a distinct position in a category without disagreeing with something the category takes for granted.

## The category assumption

Every category has an assumption. Something that everyone in the space takes as given, organizes their product around, and communicates as the baseline truth about how the problem gets solved.

The assumption might be correct. It might be partly wrong. It might be entirely wrong but widely held because nobody has done the work to challenge it.

Your job, in positioning, is to identify that assumption and say clearly why you think it's wrong.

This is harder than it sounds because the assumption is often invisible to people inside the category. It's so taken for granted that it feels like reality rather than a choice.

## How to find yours

Ask your best customers why the last solution they tried didn't work.

Not the surface answer — "it was too complicated," "we didn't have time to implement it." The real answer. What was the assumption behind the product that turned out not to be true for them?

That assumption is what you're disagreeing with. Build your positioning around the reframe.

## What it looks like

A consultancy that disagrees with the assumption that strategy and execution should be separated. (One person who does both.)

A software company that disagrees with the assumption that the problem is too little data. (The problem is not knowing which data to act on.)

A copywriter who disagrees with the assumption that brand voice is a tone guide. (It's the residue of someone with an opinion making decisions.)

Each of these is a fight. Each of them says: the category believes X, and we believe the opposite is true. That's a position. Positions convert.

*I ask clients what they disagree with in their category before I write a word of their copy. The answer is the most useful thing they'll give me.*
