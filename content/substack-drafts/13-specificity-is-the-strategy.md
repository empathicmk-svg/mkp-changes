Not a detail. The whole strategy.

When people talk about brand strategy, they usually mean: market segmentation, positioning frameworks, competitive analysis, ICP development. These are useful tools. They are not the strategy.

The strategy is whether your copy names the right problem, for the right person, with enough specificity that the right person feels recognized.

Everything else is infrastructure.

## Why specificity is the move

The human brain is wired to filter out generic information. This is not a metaphor — it's the mechanism by which the nervous system manages the volume of input it receives. Familiar patterns get suppressed. Novel, specific, relevant information gets attention.

"We help companies grow revenue" is familiar. It patterns-matches to a hundred other things your prospect has read. It gets suppressed.

"We help companies identify the 20% of their messaging that's suppressing 60% of their conversions" is specific. It doesn't pattern-match. It requires processing.

Specific copy gets processed. Generic copy gets skimmed.

## The objection I hear

"But we serve a broad market. We can't be that specific without excluding people."

Here's what actually happens when you're that specific: the right people feel seen and engage more deeply. The wrong people self-select out faster. Your overall conversion rate goes up because the visitors who convert are the ones you can actually serve.

Specificity doesn't reduce volume. It improves signal. That's a good trade.

## How to get more specific

Take your current homepage positioning statement. Then answer: specific to whom? Specific to what problem? Specific to what outcome?

If you can't answer all three, the positioning isn't specific enough. Keep going until you can.

Specificity isn't about adding words. It's about cutting the ones that could belong to anyone.

---

*The single most common change I make to copy is removing the generic qualifier and replacing it with the specific truth. It almost always makes the sentence shorter.*
