It doesn't hurt all at once. That's why it persists.

Bad copy — genuinely bad, confusing, grammatically broken copy — is easy to fix because it's obvious. Someone reads it and says: this is a problem. Fix it.

Generic copy is different. Generic copy reads fine. It's professional. It sounds like a company. Nobody's embarrassed by it. So it stays on the page for years while the company grows past it.

And the whole time it's there, it's doing its quiet damage.

## The damage generic copy does

It attracts the wrong traffic. When your copy describes the category instead of the specific position, you attract everyone interested in the category. Most of them are not your customer. They bounce. Your analytics look busy. Your conversions look flat.

It extends sales cycles. Prospects who arrive without a clear understanding of what you specifically do have to do that work in the sales conversation. The sales rep becomes the interpreter of the website, which is slow and expensive and depends on having a very good sales rep.

It suppresses price. When prospects can't see the differentiation, they compare on price. Generic copy guarantees price competition because it fails to show what you're worth relative to the alternatives.

It obscures fit. The wrong clients get through. They seemed like a match because the copy was broad enough to include them. The engagement is hard. The renewal is uncertain.

## Why it doesn't get fixed

Because no single week looks like a crisis. The drip of wrong-fit traffic and mediocre conversion rates is the background noise of the business. It doesn't trigger an alarm. It just sets the ceiling a little lower than it needs to be.

## The fix

Name the specific thing. For the specific person. In the first sentence.

That's it. The rest follows from that.

---

*The cost of generic copy is almost always larger than the cost of fixing it. That math, once you run it, is pretty clarifying.*
