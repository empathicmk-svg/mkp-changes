You're not spending too much on ads. You're spending too little on clarity.

When CAC climbs, most growth teams look at channel mix. They test new audiences. They rotate creatives. They hire another media buyer. They do everything except ask the question that actually explains the number.

Why isn't the ad working?

Not the format. Not the targeting. The message.

## The diagnosis nobody runs

There is a specific sentence that explains your positioning problem. It sounds like this:

*"We help [broad category] do [generic outcome] with [feature list]."*

You know this sentence. You wrote it. It's on your homepage, your LinkedIn, your pitch deck. It sounds professional. It says nothing.

When your ad leads with that sentence — or the feeling of that sentence — the visitor doesn't know if they've found what they were looking for. So they leave. And you paid for that click.

CAC is what happens when visitors can't immediately confirm they're in the right place.

## What positioning actually does

Positioning is pre-selection. It says to the right people: you belong here. And to everyone else: this is not for you.

Most companies are afraid of the second part. So they write for everyone. And everyone scrolls past.

The irony is that the narrower your positioning, the lower your CAC. Not because you're running to a smaller market, but because the people who find you immediately recognize themselves. They don't need to be convinced. They just need to be met with accuracy.

## The question that fixes it

Before you rotate another creative, answer this:

What specific thing does your best customer believe about their problem before they find you — and does your homepage reflect that belief back at them in the first sentence?

If you can't answer the first part, you need positioning work.
If you can answer the first part but your homepage doesn't reflect it, you need copy work.

The difference matters. One is strategy. One is execution. Running execution without the strategy is why your CAC keeps climbing.
