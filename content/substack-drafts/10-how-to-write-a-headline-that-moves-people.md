Not that stops them. Moves them.

Stopping is the lower bar. A provocative claim stops people. So does a shocking image. So does a number. Stopping is a reflex.

Moving is different. Moving means the reader goes from where they were — static, evaluating, uncertain — to somewhere new. They feel the need to read the next sentence. Then the one after. They are in motion.

A headline that moves people accomplishes this by saying something true that the reader hadn't put into words yet.

## The anatomy of a headline that works

There are formulas. The formulas produce acceptable headlines. They do not produce headlines that move people.

What produces a headline that moves people is specificity pointed at a real belief.

"Grow your business faster" is not specific and does not name a belief. It names a desire, which is different. Desire is generic. Belief is particular.

Here's the distinction: desire is what the prospect wants. Belief is what they already think is true about why they haven't gotten there yet.

"Your copy is writing checks your product can't cash" names a belief. The reader who has wondered why their conversion rate is stuck while the product gets better — they feel that headline.

## The diagnostic question

Before you write the headline, answer this: what does your best customer believe is the reason they haven't solved this problem yet?

Their belief is probably partially right and partially wrong. Your job is to name the part that's right — that's what creates the recognition — and then redirect them toward the part that's wrong. The headline names the problem. The subheadline offers the reframe.

That structure is a headline that moves people.

## The test

Write five versions. Read each one and ask: would a stranger feel recognized by this, or informed by it?

Informed is a fine feeling. Recognized is the feeling that makes someone read.

Keep writing until you have one that recognizes.
