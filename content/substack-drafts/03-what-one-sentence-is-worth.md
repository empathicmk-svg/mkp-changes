Every piece of copy has a first sentence. Most companies treat it as an introduction. It isn't.

The first sentence is the filter. It determines whether the next sentence gets read. Which determines whether the paragraph gets read. Which determines whether the page gets read. Which determines whether the person schedules a call.

The entire funnel sits downstream of one sentence.

## What first sentences actually do

A first sentence earns attention. It does this one of three ways:

It names a problem the reader has but hasn't articulated yet.
It says something they believe but haven't heard said plainly before.
It makes a claim specific enough to create curiosity.

What it cannot do is introduce the company. That is not interesting to anyone who doesn't already know the company. "We are a software company that helps teams work better" is a statement about you. The reader doesn't care about you yet. They care about their problem.

## The math behind the sentence

If your homepage gets 10,000 visits a month and 40% of them leave within five seconds, you're losing 4,000 qualified visitors before they read a single word.

You can't fix that with a better second paragraph. The first sentence has to do more work.

A first sentence that stops one more out of ten of those early exits adds 400 engaged visitors monthly. At a typical B2B SaaS conversion rate, that's not nothing. That's deals.

## How to write it

Answer this question in plain language: what is the specific thing your best customer is struggling with when they find you?

Not the category. The specific thing.

Then write a sentence that names that thing so accurately they stop and feel recognized.

That sentence is worth more than anything else on the page.
