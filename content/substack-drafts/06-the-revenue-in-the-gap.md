Most companies leak money in the space between what they've become and how they still describe themselves.

This happens at every stage. The startup that was a "project management tool" in 2021 is now something meaningfully different — but the homepage still says project management tool because nobody has had the time or the nerve to change it.

The product has moved. The team has learned. The customers have clarified what they're actually buying. But the words are still pointing at the old version of the company.

That gap is where revenue goes.

## How the gap forms

It forms slowly, in the normal course of business.

You ship features. You talk to customers. You lose some deals and win others and develop opinions about why. Over 18 months, the company's real understanding of its market sharpens considerably.

None of that sharpening makes it onto the website. Because updating the website requires someone to sit down and decide what the new version of the company actually is, and that's harder than shipping features and easier to defer.

So the gap widens. The copy describes version 1.0. The product is on version 3.4. Prospects arrive expecting one thing and get something harder to categorize. Some figure it out. Many don't stay long enough to try.

## What closing the gap is worth

The prospects who are the best fit for version 3.4 are finding a homepage that describes version 1.0. Some of them leave. The ones who stay take longer to convert because they have to do interpretive work you should have done for them.

The gap costs you both. It costs you the visitors who self-select out incorrectly, and it costs you weeks of sales cycle on the ones who stay but don't immediately understand what they're looking at.

## The audit

Pull a list of your five most recent clients. Read what they actually bought, what problem they actually had, what made them choose you.

Now read your homepage.

How much of what those five clients bought is reflected in your current copy? If the answer is less than half, you have a gap.

The size of the gap correlates directly with the amount of revenue you're leaving in it.
