Small conversion improvements sound incremental until you run them forward.

0.3 percentage points is not a dramatic number. It does not sound like the kind of improvement that changes a business. It sounds like noise, like the kind of variance you'd see between two weeks of normal traffic.

It isn't.

## What 0.3% compounds into

Here's a model. A B2B SaaS company with 8,000 monthly visitors, a 1.4% conversion rate, and a $24,000 average contract value.

At 1.4%: 112 conversions per month. $2.688M annually.
At 1.7%: 136 conversions per month. $3.264M annually.

The difference is $576,000 per year. From 0.3 percentage points.

That is not a nice-to-have. That is a rounding error in the cost of fixing the copy that was suppressing the rate.

## Why this number doesn't get the attention it deserves

Because the cost of weak copy is invisible. It doesn't appear as a line item. It doesn't trigger a notification. It just quietly limits what would have happened if the page had worked better.

The $576,000 doesn't show up as a loss. It just never shows up at all. That makes it easy to ignore.

This is why founders keep spending on channels and ads and SDRs — the costs of those things are visible — while deferring the copy work that would make all of those channels more effective.

## The multiplier logic

Here's the other way to think about it.

Every dollar you spend on traffic is multiplied by your conversion rate. 1.4% is your current multiplier. You could increase the traffic budget by 20% and add 1,600 visitors per month at 1.4% — which gives you 22 additional conversions.

Or you could fix the copy. Add 0.3 points to the rate. Same 8,000 visitors, 24 more conversions.

Same output. One requires more spend. One requires better language.

The spend is faster. The language compounds.
