The writing you do when no one is watching is what makes the public writing matter.

There is a temptation, when building a public voice, to start with the public. To draft the newsletter post, the LinkedIn article, the bylined piece. To treat every piece of writing as an output to be optimized.

This is the wrong order of operations. It produces content. It doesn't produce voice.

## What private writing does

Private writing does two things that public writing cannot.

First, it lets you be wrong. When you're writing for no one, you can follow a thought to its actual conclusion without managing how it sounds along the way. You can write what you actually believe before you know whether it's defensible. The defensibility comes later. The belief has to come first.

Second, it builds a reservoir. Public writing is the withdrawal. Private writing is the deposit. When the deposits are small or infrequent, public writing feels like squeezing. When the deposits are regular and honest, public writing feels like selecting from abundance.

## What it looks like

It can be ten minutes in the morning. It can be a page a week. It can be shorter.

The format doesn't matter. The constraints do:

No audience. You are not performing for future-you. You are not writing a draft.
No hedging. Write what you think, not the diplomatic version.
No stopping at discomfort. The discomfort is usually where the interesting thing is.

If you find you're writing something you'd be embarrassed to publish, that's a signal, not a problem. That's your real view, unvarnished. Mine it.

## The result

After six months of consistent private writing, most people find their public voice has sharpened considerably. Not because the public writing got more practice, but because the thinking underneath it got more honest.

Voice is not a product of more writing. It's a product of more honest writing.
