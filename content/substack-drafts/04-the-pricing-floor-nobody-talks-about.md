Your copy is setting a price ceiling. You may not have noticed.

Pricing conversations are usually treated as a function of market research, competitive benchmarking, or willingness-to-pay surveys. These are useful. They're also missing the variable that often determines whether a prospect accepts your price or balks at it.

The variable is perceived differentiation.

## How copy sets the floor

When a prospect can't see the specific way you're different from the other three vendors on their shortlist, they price-compare. That's rational. That's what you do when all things appear equal.

Copy that reads like everyone else's copy tells the prospect that all things are equal. So they negotiate down. Or they choose on price. Or they stall, waiting to see if you'll flex.

The floor isn't set by your production costs. It's set by how clearly the reader understands why you're the only choice for their specific problem.

## The differentiation that copy conveys

Differentiation isn't a feature list. Prospects have read your feature list and the competitor's feature list and they look about the same.

Differentiation is a point of view. It's the specific way you see the problem differently than everyone else in the market. It's the thing you'd be willing to argue about at dinner.

When your copy communicates that — clearly, specifically, without hedging — the prospect isn't comparing you to alternatives anymore. They're evaluating whether you understand their problem well enough to be trusted with it.

That evaluation has a different outcome than a price comparison.

## What to do with this

Pull your current positioning statement. The one on the homepage, above the fold.

Ask: does this explain how we see the problem differently? Or does it describe the category we're in and the outcome we provide?

If it's the second, your copy is compressing your price. The fix is not a different price. It's a different sentence.
