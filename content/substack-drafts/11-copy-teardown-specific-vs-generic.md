Two versions of the same page. One converts. Here's why.

I'm going to walk through a real pattern I see constantly — one composite, no names — because the difference between the two versions is more instructive than most copy advice.

## Version A (the version that doesn't work)

> **We help B2B companies accelerate their go-to-market strategy and drive revenue growth through proven sales and marketing alignment.**
>
> Our expert team brings decades of experience helping companies like yours reach their growth potential. We offer a full suite of solutions including messaging strategy, sales enablement, and content creation.
>
> Ready to transform your business? Book a call today.

This copy fails in a specific, diagnosable way. It describes a category. It signals that the company exists and has services. It gives the prospect no reason to believe this is different from the other eleven vendors they've evaluated.

The reader's response: fine. Let me look at the pricing.

## Version B (the version that works)

> **Your pipeline is full. Your conversion rate says otherwise. The problem isn't your product — it's the 14 words on your homepage that are describing the wrong customer.**
>
> Most B2B companies over-invest in lead generation and under-invest in the messaging that determines what those leads do when they arrive. I fix the second part.
>
> One engagement. One senior operator. No agency layers. [Book the conversation.](#)

Same services. Completely different result.

## What changed

Version B does three things Version A doesn't:

**It names a specific failure mode** the reader may recognize. "Your pipeline is full. Your conversion rate says otherwise." That's a real problem with a specific shape.

**It names the cause with precision.** "14 words on your homepage describing the wrong customer." Not a general positioning problem — a specific, fixable thing.

**It differentiates on operating model.** "One senior operator. No agency layers." This is the sentence that separates this company from every agency. It's one sentence. It does significant work.

## The lesson

Generic copy describes the category. Specific copy names the problem, names the cause, and names what makes you the right solution.

The reader doesn't need to be convinced that the category is valuable. They already believe that. They need to be convinced that you're the specific right answer to their specific problem.

That's what specific copy does.

*When I audit a client's homepage, this is the lens I use. Category copy vs. problem copy. The gap between the two is always measurable.*
