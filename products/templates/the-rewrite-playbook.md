## Part 1 — The Story Audit

Before you write a word of copy, you find the story. Most people skip this and end up polishing a story that was never theirs to begin with. The audit runs in three layers: career, identity, pivot.

### Layer 1 — The Career Audit

Don't list jobs. List *turns* — the moments the work changed you or you changed it.

```
THE TURNS
  For each significant role/project, answer:

  - What was actually broken when I arrived? ____________
  - What did I do that someone else wouldn't have? ____________
  - What's the number, name, or outcome that proves it? ____________
  - What did I learn that I still use? ____________
  - What did I leave behind / outgrow here? ____________
```

Run this for your last three or four meaningful roles. You're hunting for the through-line — the thing you keep doing across every job, regardless of title. That's your spine.

```
THE THROUGH-LINE
  Across every role, the thing I keep doing is: ____________
  The kind of problem I'm always the one to solve: ____________
  The skill I have that my title never named: ____________
```

### Layer 2 — The Identity Audit

Title is what you were paid to do. Identity is what you *are*. They're rarely the same, and the gap is usually where you're being underestimated.

```
  My official title(s): ____________
  What I actually do all day: ____________
  What people interrupt me to ask for: ____________
  The thing I'm proud of that's nowhere on my résumé: ____________
  The word people use about my work that I'd never pick: ____________
  The word I'd pick: ____________
```

### Layer 3 — The Pivot Narrative

If you're at a turn, the page has to explain the move — without sounding like an apology. The trap is the "I'm reinventing myself!" energy that reads as lost. You're not reinventing. You're continuing. Frame the pivot as the logical next step the through-line was always heading toward.

```
THE PIVOT FRAME
  I'm moving from ____________ to ____________.

  The thing those two have in common (the through-line): ___________

  The pivot isn't a break from my story — it's the part where: _______

  What I'm NOT leaving behind (the transferable core): ____________
```

### Worked example — the full audit

```
CAREER through-line:
  "I keep getting handed broken processes and turning them into
  systems people actually follow. Did it in ops, did it in product,
  did it in the nonprofit. Title changed every time. The work didn't."

IDENTITY:
  Official title: "Senior Program Manager."
  Actually do: design the system, then get humans to trust it.
  People interrupt me for: "how do we make this not chaos."
  Proud of, not on résumé: rewrote onboarding, cut ramp time in half.
  Word others use: "organized." Word I'd pick: "calm under mess."

PIVOT:
  Moving from program management to operations consulting.
  In common: making broken things run without burning people out.
  The pivot is the part where I do it for many companies, not one.
  Not leaving behind: the human side. Systems people hate don't work.
```

Look at what the audit produced. Not a job history — a *thesis*. "I make broken things run without burning people out." Everything downstream gets written from that line.

## Part 2 — The Positioning Statement Builder

This is the load-bearing sentence. Get it right and the LinkedIn, the bio, the pitch all write themselves from it. Get it wrong and you'll keep producing copy that doesn't quite fit.

A positioning statement isn't a tagline. It's an internal tool — the truest, sharpest version of what you do, for whom, and why it matters. You may never publish it word for word. But everything you publish should be downstream of it.

### The builder

```
  I help [SPECIFIC PEOPLE]
  [DO / GET A SPECIFIC OUTCOME]
  by [YOUR DISTINCT APPROACH / THE THING ONLY YOU DO],
  so that [THE DEEPER STAKE — what it really changes for them].

  Unlike [THE DEFAULT ALTERNATIVE], I [THE KEY DIFFERENCE].
```

### The four tests

Run your draft through these. Fail one, rewrite.

- [ ] **The specific test.** Could a competitor say the exact same sentence? If yes, it's not positioning — it's a category. Sharpen until it's yours alone.
- [ ] **The outcome test.** Does it name what *changes* for the client, or just what you do? "I write copy" fails. "I make people sound like the smartest version of themselves" passes.
- [ ] **The proof test.** Can you back the claim with one piece of evidence? If not, either find the proof or lower the claim.
- [ ] **The mouth test.** Could you say it out loud to a stranger without cringing? If it only works on paper, it's too stiff.

### Worked example

```
  WEAK (category, not position):
    "I'm an operations consultant who helps companies improve
    their processes and increase efficiency."

  STRONG (built from the audit thesis):
    "I help fast-growing teams whose ops have turned to chaos
    rebuild the systems — without making everyone hate the systems.
    Most consultants leave you a 40-page playbook nobody opens.
    I leave you something your team actually uses on a Monday."
```

Keep this somewhere visible. It's the ruler you measure every other piece of copy against for the rest of the rewrite.

## Part 3 — The LinkedIn Rebuild

LinkedIn is where you're read most and edited least. We rebuild it section by section, top to bottom, each one earning the scroll to the next.

### The Headline

Not your job title. Your title is in the box below your name already — repeating it wastes the most valuable line on the profile. The headline is the value you create, in language a human searches and a human understands.

```
  FORMULA A (outcome-led):
    [What you do for whom] | [proof or specialty] | [optional: status/seeking]

  FORMULA B (identity-led):
    [The role you want to own] who [the distinct thing you do]
```

```
  WEAK:   "Senior Program Manager at Acme Corp"
  STRONG: "I rebuild the ops that chaos broke — without the 40-page
           playbook nobody reads | Ops consulting for scaling teams"
```

### The "About" Section

This is the heart of the rebuild. LinkedIn truncates after ~3 lines on mobile — so the opening must stand alone and pull the click. Write it in first person. Write it like you talk.

```
  STRUCTURE:
  [HOOK — one line that stands alone and earns the "see more" click]

  [THE TURN — what changed, what you saw, why you do this. 2–4 lines.]

  [THE PROOF — specific results. Numbers, names, outcomes. 2–3 lines.]

  [THE NOW — what you're doing today and who it's for. 2–3 lines.]

  [THE ASK — one clear next step. How to reach you / what to do.]
```

```
  WORKED EXAMPLE:

  Hook:
    Every company I've walked into was drowning in its own processes.

  The turn:
    I used to think the fix was a better tool. It's not. It's a system
    simple enough that people use it when no one's watching. After a
    decade of building those inside companies, I started building them
    for companies — and the difference is in who actually adopts them.

  The proof:
    At my last role I cut new-hire ramp time from 8 weeks to 4 and
    killed three redundant approval layers nobody missed. The teams
    didn't just tolerate the new system. They defended it.

  The now:
    I work with scaling teams — usually 20 to 100 people — at the point
    where the duct tape stops holding. We rebuild the core operating
    systems so growth stops breaking things.

  The ask:
    If your ops feel like chaos with a calendar, let's talk.
    DM me or reach me at mkparrish.com.
```

### The Experience Entries

Stop pasting the job description. Each entry is a mini-story: the broken thing, what you did, the result. Lead every bullet with a verb and end on proof.

```
  ENTRY STRUCTURE:
  [One line of context — what the role/company actually was]
  • [Strong verb] [specific action] → [measurable result]
  • [Strong verb] [specific action] → [measurable result]
  • [Strong verb] [specific action] → [measurable result]

  EXAMPLE:
  Owned ops for a 60-person SaaS scaling past its processes.
  • Redesigned onboarding → cut ramp time 8 weeks to 4
  • Killed 3 redundant approval layers → 30% faster shipping
  • Built the playbook the next two hires were trained on
```

### The rest of the profile — quick checklist

- [ ] **Photo:** current, clear, looks like the person who'd show up to the meeting.
- [ ] **Banner:** says or shows what you do — not a stock mountain.
- [ ] **Featured:** 2–4 pieces of real proof — work samples, a post that landed, your site.
- [ ] **Skills:** the ones that match the position you want, not every skill you have.
- [ ] **Custom URL:** linkedin.com/in/yourname — not the auto-generated string.
- [ ] **Open to / Services:** set if you want inbound. Off if you don't.

## Part 4 — The Bio Rewrites

You need your story at multiple lengths. Write the longest first, then cut down — never the reverse, or the short versions come out hollow. All four pull from the same positioning statement, so they sound like one person.

### The 25-word bio (the bottom-of-an-email, the speaker intro)

```
  TEMPLATE:
    [Name] [does the specific valuable thing] for [specific people].
    [One line of proof or what they're known for].

  EXAMPLE:
    MK Parrish rewrites the way ambitious people are read on the page —
    bios, sites, and LinkedIn profiles that finally match the person.
```

### The 50-word bio (the website "about" blurb, the directory listing)

```
  TEMPLATE:
    [Name] [does the thing] for [people]. [Approach — the distinct how].
    [Proof line — a result, a name, a number]. [The now / the ask].

  EXAMPLE:
    Jordan Rivera rebuilds the operations that growth breaks — for teams
    of 20 to 100 stuck in their own processes. Where most consultants
    leave a playbook nobody opens, Jordan leaves systems teams actually
    use. He cut one client's onboarding time in half. He's at jordan.co.
```

### The 100-word bio (the conference program, the guest-post byline)

```
  STRUCTURE:
    [Lead with the one thing you're known for — not your title.]
    [The through-line: what you keep doing across roles.]
    [Two beats of specific proof.]
    [What you're doing now and who it's for.]
    [Where to find you.]
```

### The first-person version (for your own site, your newsletter)

Same content, but spoken. Contractions. The voice you'd use at the table, not the podium. Take the 50-word bio and rewrite it as "I" — then cut anything that sounds like you're reading it off a card.

```
  EXAMPLE:
    I rebuild the operations that growth breaks. I've spent a decade
    walking into companies drowning in their own processes and leaving
    them with systems simple enough that people actually use them. I cut
    one team's onboarding time in half — and they defended the change.
    Now I do it for scaling teams across the board. Find me at jordan.co.
```

## The Rewrite checklist — before you hit publish

- [ ] Story audit done — you can state your through-line in one sentence
- [ ] Positioning statement passes all four tests
- [ ] Headline sells value, not title
- [ ] "About" hook stands alone and earns the click
- [ ] Every experience bullet ends on proof
- [ ] All four bios trace back to the positioning statement
- [ ] Read the whole profile out loud — sounds like you, not a category
- [ ] One person who knows you read it and said "yes, that's you"
- [ ] Pivot (if any) reads as continuation, never apology
