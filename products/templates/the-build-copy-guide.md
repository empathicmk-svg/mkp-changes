Building a website is not primarily a design problem. It's a copy problem. Most sites fail at the sentence level — the headline says nothing, the about page talks only about the company, the services page lists features with no outcomes. A beautiful site with weak copy is a beautiful building with no address.

## Section One: Before You Write Anything

### The Positioning Brief

Five questions. Every word on the site comes from the answers. Write them down before you touch a single page.

1. **Who is this for?** One specific person, not a category.
2. **What's the one thing you do better or differently than anyone else?**
3. **What does the reader most need to believe to hire you?**
4. **What's the single most common objection — and the honest answer to it?**
5. **What do you want them to do right now?**

**Worked example (a boutique financial-modeling firm):**
*For: a non-financial seed-stage founder prepping to raise. Better/different: we build the model AND teach you to defend it in the room. Need to believe: this won't be generic spreadsheet theater. Top objection: "I can find a cheaper template" → answer: a template can't answer an investor's follow-up; we build for the questions, not the cells. Action: book a 20-minute fit call.*

### The Voice Snapshot

Three adjectives, three banned words, one "sounds like us" sentence. This is what keeps the site from reading like a committee wrote it.

**Fill-in:** *We sound ______, ______, ______. We never say ______, ______, ______. At our best we write lines like: "______________."*

## Section Two: The Homepage

One job: convince the right person they're in the right place, and point them to one next step.

### The Hero

- **H1 formula:** *[What you do] for [who]* — with a verb, not a noun. ✅ "We build financial models for early-stage founders." ❌ "Financial Strategy Solutions." (A noun-pile says nothing; a verb makes a promise.)
- **Subhead formula:** the specific outcome your best clients get, in one sentence. *"Walk into your raise able to defend every number — not just present it."*
- **CTA:** use a verb that names the next step. **"Book a fit call"** / **"See the work"** / **"Get the model."** Never "Learn More" — it commits the reader to nothing and converts nothing.

### The Social Proof Strip

Three kinds of proof, in order of power: **specific outcomes** ("closed a $3M seed six weeks after we built the model"), **named quotes** (with company + title), **recognizable logos.** One line of real outcome beats five vague testimonials.

### The Services Preview — the three-card rule

No more than three cards on the homepage. Each card: a one-line title, a one-line *outcome*, one CTA.
*Example card — "The Raise Model · An investor-ready model you can defend in the room · See how it works →"*

### The About Preview

One paragraph, written about what you *believe* — not who you are. It's a conviction statement, not a bio.
*Example: "We think most founders lose the room not on the idea but on the math they can't defend. So we build the math with you, until the answer to every follow-up is already in your head."*

### The Final CTA

Restate the offer simply, then add the friction-reducer that answers the last hesitation before the click.
*Example: "Book a 20-minute fit call. If we're not the right fit, we'll tell you on the call and point you somewhere better."*

## Section Three: The Services Pages

A services page fails one of two ways: features with no outcomes, or outcomes so vague it forgets to say what you actually get. Use this frame per service:

- **Header:** service name + one-line outcome.
- **The problem:** 3–4 sentences describing *their* situation, in their words, not your pitch.
- **The solution:** what you do and what it produces, concretely. *"You get X, Y, and Z. Within [timeframe] you'll have [deliverable]."*
- **The process (optional):** 3–5 steps. Makes the abstract feel real and de-risks the buy.
- **The proof:** one case or quote specific to *this* service.
- **The CTA:** price anchor ("from $X") + booking link.

**Worked mini-example — "The Raise Model":**
*Problem: "You're weeks from a raise with a spreadsheet you didn't build and can't fully defend. The model looks fine until an investor asks why churn flattens in month 14." Solution: "We rebuild your model with you — assumptions you can explain, scenarios you can run live, and a one-page summary that answers the five questions investors always ask. In two weeks you'll have a model and the fluency to defend it." Proof: [client quote]. CTA: "From $6,000 · Book a fit call →"*

## Section Four: The About Page

The most miswritten page on the internet. Most About pages are about the company; the best are about the reader, told through how you came to care. Six parts:

1. **The empathy opener** — start with *them.* Name the situation they're in.
2. **The bridge** — how you came to understand that situation. The true version.
3. **The conviction** — what you believe that shapes the work. A point of view, not a values list.
4. **The credentials** — brief, specific, ordered by relevance, not prestige.
5. **The human detail** — one non-professional thing. People buy from people.
6. **The CTA** — one next step.

**Annotated opener example:** *"Most founders don't lose the raise on the idea. They lose it on a number they can't defend at 4pm in a cold conference room. [empathy] I learned that the hard way, sitting in those rooms as the analyst who watched great companies fumble the math. [bridge] So I stopped building models *for* founders and started building them *with* founders — because the model isn't the deliverable; the confidence is. [conviction]"*

## Section Five: The Contact & Booking Pages

The last page before they decide — and the one most likely to abandon them at the worst moment.

- **The micro-headline:** one line that justifies the friction of the form. *"Tell us where you are in your raise — we'll reply within one business day."*
- **The expectation-setter:** say exactly what happens next, with a timeline. *"You'll get a real reply from MK (not a bot) within 24 hours, with two times to talk."*
- **Form fields:** include only what you'll act on. Every extra field lowers completion. Name, email, one open question usually beats a ten-field interrogation.
- **The booking page:** warm the conversation above the embed. *"This is a 20-minute fit call. Come with one question about your raise; leave knowing whether we can help."*

---

## Section Six: The One Test

Run every sentence on the site through one question:

> *Does this sentence help the right person take the right action — or does it just make us feel good?*

❌ Feels good: "We are a leading provider of integrated strategic financial solutions."
✅ Helps them: "We build the investor model you can actually defend in the room."

It's not an elegant test. It's just useful. Run all of it through before you publish — the homepage hero first, the About second, because those are where the feel-good sentences hide.
