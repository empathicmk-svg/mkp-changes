## Part One: Before You Launch Anything

### The clarity check
You cannot launch what you haven't named. Before this book does anything else, it walks through five quick tests your offer has to pass. Not to slow you down — to prevent the most expensive mistake faith creators make: launching something undercooked to protect the feeling of having launched.

**The five pre-launch tests:**
1. *Can you say the transformation in one sentence without using the word "journey"?*
2. *Have at least three people in your exact target audience said they would pay for this at your price?*
3. *Can you fulfill it this month if ten people bought tomorrow?*
4. *Do you have a way to collect payment and deliver the product that isn't "DM me and I'll Venmo you"?*
5. *Do you believe, in your gut, that this is priced fairly — not just for the market but for what it's worth?*

If any of those is a no, the launch date moves until it's a yes.

### The pre-launch window
Eight to twelve weeks before doors open, you build the context. Not hype — context. The content that helps your audience understand the problem your offer solves, told from genuine personal conviction. This section includes a pre-launch content calendar template (12 weeks, mapped by content type) and the three pre-launch email sequences you actually need.

## Part Two: The Launch

### Launch architecture
The four-week launch arc (matching Module 6 of Scripture & Strategy but with full templates):

**Week 1 — The Problem Week**: Content that surfaces the exact pain your offer addresses. Three content pieces, one email, mapped.
**Week 2 — The Possibility Week**: Before/after framing. What does life look like after the transformation? Three content pieces, one email.
**Week 3 — The Open Week**: Doors open Monday. Email sequence mapped across 7 days (open, middle, close). FAQ post. Social proof if you have it; conviction if you don't.
**Week 4 — The Follow-Through Week**: One "last chance" email (not manipulative — just honest about the close). One post about what early buyers are already saying. Doors close. Done.

Every email in this playbook is a complete template — not a framework you fill in yourself, but a draft with real language you can keep or rewrite. The language sounds like a person, not a funnel.

### Pricing the launch
Special launch pricing, early-bird windows, payment plans — when to use them, how to frame them without false urgency, and how to close without discounting your way into resentment. Includes a pricing decision tree.

### The honest launch metrics
What to track (and what to ignore):
- Email open rate and click rate on launch emails
- Conversion rate from click to checkout
- Where the buyers came from (organic content, email, referral)
- Abandoned checkouts (this number is usually higher and more useful than people expect)

Not to track during the launch: follower count, impressions, engagement rate. These are vanity during a launch week. Revenue per email subscriber is the number.

## Part Three: After the Launch

### The debrief
The launch debrief is the most skipped step and the most valuable. Twelve questions to answer within 48 hours of closing:

1. How many units sold?
2. At what revenue?
3. What was the email list size at open?
4. What was the overall conversion rate (buyers ÷ list)?
5. Which email had the highest click-to-purchase rate?
6. Where did the buyers come from?
7. What was the most common objection you encountered (from replies, DMs, or your own read of the silence)?
8. What would you price it differently?
9. What content piece drove the most traffic to the sales page?
10. What would you cut from the launch sequence next time?
11. What would you add?
12. What did the work teach you about who this is actually for?

This debrief becomes the brief for the next launch. A creator who debriefs every launch compounds their results. One who doesn't starts from scratch every time.

### Evergreen after launch
Most digital products shouldn't retire after the launch window closes. This section covers converting a launched product to evergreen — a sales page that's always open, an email automation that introduces new subscribers to the offer, and a content drip that keeps it visible without a full launch every time.

## Part Four: Revenue Architecture

### The recurring revenue layer
Single launches are feast-and-famine. Recurring revenue is the antidote. Three models that work for faith-based creators:

1. **The monthly membership**: A lower-ticket ($19–$49/month) container — live calls, community, exclusive content. Works when you have an audience with a recurring need.
2. **The subscription newsletter**: A paid Substack or Beehiiv newsletter ($9–$15/month). Works when your content is the product.
3. **The monthly retainer**: A small number of premium clients on a recurring monthly engagement. Works when you offer ongoing strategy, coaching, or ghostwriting.

Each model includes a launch framework, a pricing guide, and the minimum viable audience size needed to make it work.

### Offer sequencing
How to introduce new offers without cannibalizing existing ones, how to sequence your ladder so each product leads naturally to the next, and when to run simultaneous offers vs. a single focused launch.

### The annual revenue plan
A simple one-page template: four launches per year, one recurring revenue stream, one premium offer. What it takes to hit Foundation-level revenue ($3K–$6K/month) and what comes after.
