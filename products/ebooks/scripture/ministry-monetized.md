## Part One: Before You Launch Anything

Most failed launches were lost here, weeks before doors opened, in the part nobody posts about. Get this part right and the launch mostly runs itself. Get it wrong and no email sequence on earth will save it.

### The Clarity Check

You cannot launch what you haven't named. Before anything else, your offer has to pass five tests. Answer each out loud, in writing. A *no* anywhere moves the launch date — that's not failure, that's the test working.

**One — Can you say the transformation in one sentence, without the word "journey"?** "I help bivocational pastors turn their sermon prep into a paid weekly newsletter." That's an offer. "A journey toward deeper purpose and abundance" is a mood. If you can't say what changes for the buyer, neither can they.

**Two — Have three real people in your exact audience said they'd pay your price?** Not "that sounds amazing" — *"yes, I would pay $42 for that."* Three. If you haven't asked, you don't have a launch yet; you have a hope. Go ask before you build.

**Three — Could you fulfill it this month if ten people bought tomorrow?** If ten sales would create a crisis, your offer is bigger than your capacity, and you'll resent the buyers. Shrink the first version until the answer is a calm yes.

**Four — Can you actually take the money and deliver the thing** without "DM me and I'll Venmo you"? A checkout link, a delivery method, a receipt. Friction at the cash register kills more launches than weak copy.

**Five — Do you believe, in your gut, it's priced fairly** — for what it's worth, not just what the market tolerates? If you secretly think it's too expensive, your copy will whisper that and your buyers will hear it. Price it where conviction and fairness meet.

### The Pre-Launch Window

Eight to twelve weeks before doors open, you build *context* — not hype. Context is the slow work of helping your audience understand the problem your offer solves, so that by the time you mention the offer, it lands as the obvious answer to a question they're already asking.

Here is the simple arc. **Weeks 1–4: name the problem.** Content that describes the pain your offer addresses, in your reader's own words, with no pitch attached. You are not selling; you are making people feel *seen.* A reader who thinks "she understands exactly what I'm dealing with" is pre-sold. **Weeks 5–8: reframe the problem.** Show that the usual solutions don't work and why — gently dismantle the conventional wisdom your offer improves on. **Weeks 9–12: hint at the path.** Begin telling the story of how you (or a client) solved it. Still no hard pitch. You're laying track so that when the launch arrives, the train has somewhere to go.

Three pre-launch emails do the heavy lifting, and here is the actual spine of each:

**Email A — The naming.** Open with a specific scene of the problem ("It's Sunday night and the study you poured your week into is about to vanish into a notebook no one will open"). Name it plainly. End with one line of hope and *no* call to action. You are earning trust, not asking for anything.

**Email B — The wrong fix.** "Here's what most people try, and here's why it leaves them more tired." Reframe. End by promising you'll show a better way soon.

**Email C — The quiet pre-announcement.** "I've built something for exactly this. Doors open [date]. Nothing to do yet — just wanted you to know it's coming." This single email, sent to a warmed list, routinely outperforms the entire launch week on a cold one.

> The launch is not where you convince people. It's where you collect the yes you earned in the eight weeks before it.

## Part Two: The Launch

The launch is a four-week arc. Every week has a job. Resist the urge to compress it into a frantic three days — the slow version converts better and costs you less of yourself.

### Launch Architecture

**Week 1 — The Problem Week.** Three pieces of content and one email, all circling the exact pain. No selling. You're raising the temperature, making the problem conscious and urgent. The buyer has to feel the cost of *not* solving it before any price feels reasonable.

**Week 2 — The Possibility Week.** Before-and-after. Paint the life on the other side of the transformation — concretely, not in adjectives. "Imagine opening your laptop Monday with the week's content already drafted from Sunday's study." Three pieces, one email. You're making the desire specific enough to act on.

**Week 3 — The Open Week.** Doors open Monday. This is the heart of the launch, and it runs on a seven-day email sequence:
- *Day 1 (open):* "It's here." What it is, who it's for, the price, the link. Short and clear.
- *Day 2 (the story):* Why you built it, told as a personal narrative. People buy the why.
- *Day 4 (objections):* The FAQ email. Answer the three real hesitations — time, money, "will this work for someone like me" — honestly.
- *Day 6 (proof or conviction):* If you have testimonials, share one in depth. If you don't, share your conviction and a guarantee. Conviction sells when proof is thin.
- *Day 7 (the close):* "Doors close tomorrow night." Honest, not manipulative. Restate the transformation and the link.

**Week 4 — The Follow-Through.** One last-chance email the morning of the close ("a few hours left, here's the link, no hard feelings if it's not your season"). One short post on what early buyers are already saying or doing. Then doors close. *Closing is a feature*, not a cruelty — it respects the people who said yes and gives the offer shape.

### A Drafted Launch Email (Day 1)

Use it, rewrite it, but notice the moves — it sounds like a person, names the price without flinching, and asks once.

> Subject: It's open — [Offer Name]
>
> For the last few weeks I've been describing a problem: [one line]. Today I'm handing you the fix.
>
> [Offer Name] is [one-sentence transformation]. It's for [who], and it is *not* for [who it isn't for] — I'd rather tell you now than take your money later.
>
> It's $[price]. You get [the two or three concrete things]. You can start [today / on [date]].
>
> [Link]
>
> If you've been waiting for the right moment, this is me telling you it's here. — [Name]

### Pricing the Launch

Launch pricing, early-bird windows, and payment plans are tools, not tricks. An early-bird tier (a real discount for the first 48 hours) rewards your most ready buyers and creates *honest* momentum. A payment plan ("3 × $16" instead of "$42") removes a real barrier for real people — use it freely; it is not a gimmick. What to avoid: fake countdown timers, "only 3 spots left" when there are infinite digital copies, and discounting so deep you resent the work. **Set the price you believe in, offer one honest reason to act now, and stop.**

### The Honest Launch Metrics

Track four numbers and ignore the rest: launch-email **open and click rate**, **click-to-checkout conversion**, **where buyers came from**, and **abandoned checkouts** (usually higher and more instructive than anyone expects — those are warm people stopped by one fixable thing). What to *ignore* during launch week: follower count, impressions, engagement rate. Those are weather. **Revenue per subscriber is the number that tells you the truth.**

## Part Three: After the Launch

### The Debrief

The launch debrief is the most-skipped step and the most valuable. Within 48 hours of closing — while it's fresh and before the story rewrites itself — answer twelve questions in writing:

1. How many units sold? 2. At what revenue? 3. What was the list size at open? 4. Overall conversion rate (buyers ÷ list)? 5. Which email had the highest click-to-purchase rate? 6. Where did buyers actually come from? 7. What was the most common objection — stated or sensed in the silence? 8. What would you price differently? 9. What content piece drove the most traffic to the sales page? 10. What would you cut from the sequence? 11. What would you add? 12. What did this teach you about who this is *actually* for?

**A creator who debriefs every launch compounds. One who doesn't starts from zero every time.** This document becomes the brief for your next launch — the single highest-leverage page you'll write all quarter.

### Going Evergreen

Most digital products shouldn't retire when the launch window closes. To convert a launched offer to evergreen: stand up a sales page that's always open, write a five-email welcome automation that introduces every new subscriber to the offer over their first two weeks, and keep one piece of content per month pointed at the sales page. The launch is the spike; evergreen is the floor that spike now sits on. Done once, it earns quietly for years.

## Part Four: Revenue Architecture

Single launches are feast and famine. Recurring revenue is the antidote — the income that arrives whether or not you launched this month.

### The Recurring Layer — Three Models

**The membership ($19–$49/month).** A container: live calls, community, a steady drip of exclusive content. Works when your audience has a *recurring* need and values access to you. Minimum viable audience: ~500 engaged people. Watch the real cost — churn. A membership lives or dies on whether month three feels as valuable as month one.

**The paid newsletter ($9–$15/month).** A paid Substack or Beehiiv tier. Works when *your writing is the product* and you can sustain a genuine cadence. Lower friction than a membership, lower price, far less delivery burden. Minimum viable audience: a free list of ~1,000 with strong open rates.

**The monthly retainer.** A handful of premium clients on ongoing strategy, coaching, or ghostwriting. Highest revenue per relationship, lowest audience requirement — three to five clients can outpace a 1,000-person membership. Works when you offer something only you can, and you're willing to stay close to the work.

You do not need all three. You need *one*, chosen for the audience and energy you actually have, run well for a year.

### Offer Sequencing & the Annual Plan

Sequence your ladder so each offer leads naturally to the next: a free lead magnet → a low-ticket product (this one) → the recurring layer → a premium offer. Don't run two big launches in the same month — they cannibalize attention and exhaust you. A sane annual rhythm for a faith-based solo creator: **four focused launches a year, one recurring stream running underneath, one premium offer available to the few who want it.** That structure — not a viral moment — is what carries you from scattered income to a dependable $3K–$6K/month, and it's the floor everything bigger is built on.
