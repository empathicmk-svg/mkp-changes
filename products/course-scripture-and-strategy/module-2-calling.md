# Module 2 — The Calling: Clarity Before Content

*4 lessons · ~45 minutes · You'll leave with a one-sentence calling statement: what you do, who it's for, and why it's you.*

You cannot build a brand, write an offer, or make content that lands if you can't say what you're for in a sentence. Vagueness isn't humility — it's just expensive. This module ends the vagueness.

---

## Lesson 2.1 — Calling is not a feeling

We've spiritualized "calling" into something so mystical that women wait years for a lightning bolt that never comes. Then they feel faithless for not knowing.

Calling, in practice, is far more ordinary and far more findable. It usually lives at the intersection of three things:

1. **What you keep coming back to** — the subject, the people, the problem you can't stop thinking about.
2. **What you're actually good at** — the thing others ask you for, often without you noticing it's a skill.
3. **What the world in front of you needs** — a real gap, in real people, that you're positioned to meet.

Frederick Buechner put it famously: your calling is where your deep gladness meets the world's deep hunger. That's not a feeling to wait for. It's an intersection to *map.* And mapping is a skill — which means it's learnable, which means you can do it this week.

> Stop waiting to feel called. Start working to get clear. Clarity is the form faithfulness takes when you're paying attention.

### Action step
List five things people consistently come to you for. Then five subjects you can't stop reading/thinking/talking about. Circle anything that appears on both lists. The overlap is your raw material.

---

## Lesson 2.2 — The clarity questions

Clarity comes from better questions, asked in writing. Answer these slowly. Don't perform — excavate.

- When have I felt most useful to another person? What was I doing?
- What problem do I understand from the inside because I lived it?
- Who was I, five years ago, who I'm now equipped to help?
- What makes me angry on someone else's behalf?
- If I could only teach one thing for the rest of my life, what would it be?
- What does Scripture keep pressing on in me — the theme that won't leave my Source File alone?

That last question matters more than the rest combined. The thread that keeps surfacing in your study is rarely random. Follow it.

### Action step
Answer all six in your Source File. Then read your answers out loud. You're listening for the sentence you didn't know you were going to say.

---

## Lesson 2.3 — The calling statement formula

Now we make it a sentence. Not a paragraph, not a mission statement that could belong to anyone — a single, specific line. Use this scaffold and then make it human:

```
I help [specific person]
[achieve specific outcome / make specific shift]
through [your particular approach],
because [the conviction underneath it].
```

**Weak:** "I help women grow in their faith and become their best selves."
(Could be anyone. Outcome is fog. No one is excluded, so no one is gripped.)

**Sharp:** "I help burned-out faith-based coaches build offers that pay them fairly, using a study-rooted clarity process, because I believe stewardship includes stewarding yourself."

Notice: a *specific* person, a *specific* outcome, a *named* method, and a conviction that picks a side. The sharp version repels the wrong people on purpose — which is exactly why the right people lean in.

### Action step
Draft your calling statement using the scaffold. Write it badly first. Then cut every word that could belong to someone else. Specificity is the edit.

---

## Lesson 2.4 — Testing it against resistance

A real calling statement survives contact with three pressures. Run yours through each:

1. **The "so what" test.** Say it to an imaginary stranger. Would they ask a follow-up question, or politely nod? If they'd nod, it's too safe.
2. **The exclusion test.** Does it clearly *not* describe some people? If it fits everyone, it grips no one. Who does yours rule out? Good.
3. **The conviction test.** Could you defend the "because" clause in a room of people who disagree? If the conviction is mush, the whole statement will drift.

A statement that passes all three will feel slightly scary to publish. That's correct. Clarity always costs the comfort of being vaguely acceptable to everyone.

### Action step
Run your statement through all three tests. Revise once. Then put it at the top of your Source File where you'll see it every day. Everything you build from here gets checked against this sentence.
