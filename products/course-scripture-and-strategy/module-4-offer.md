# Module 4 — The Offer: Something Worth Paying For

*4 lessons · ~50 minutes · You'll leave with at least one real, priced offer — built on your calling and ready to sell.*

Now we build the thing. Not before clarity, not before voice — now, when it has a foundation. This is where stewardship and strategy meet most directly, because nothing tests your beliefs about money and worth like naming a price out loud.

---

## Lesson 4.1 — Monetization without manipulation

Let's name the fear directly: you're afraid that charging for faith-adjacent work makes you a fraud, a Pharisee, a merchant in the temple.

So let's separate two things that get fused. **Manipulation** is using fear, false scarcity, or spiritual pressure to make someone buy something that doesn't serve them. That's the temple-merchant problem, and it's real. **Exchange** is offering something genuinely valuable and being paid fairly for it. That's not just permitted — it's the model running through all of Scripture, where the worker is worthy of their wages and the one who refuses to provide for their household has, in Paul's startling phrase, "denied the faith."

The line isn't *whether* you charge. The line is *how.* Lead with the person's good. Price for sustainability so you can keep serving. Never make someone feel spiritually deficient for not buying. Do that, and you can charge with a clear conscience and a straight spine.

> Undercharging is not humility. It's often just fear wearing humility's coat. The most generous thing you can do is build something sustainable enough to still exist next year.

### Action step
Write your honest belief about money and your work — the real one, not the polished one. Then write the truer sentence you want to operate from. Tape the second one up.

---

## Lesson 4.2 — The offer ladder

You don't need a big complicated suite. You need a clear ladder — a few offers at different prices and depths, so people can start where they are.

A simple, proven shape:

- **Free** — your content (Module 5). Builds trust, demonstrates the voice.
- **Entry ($15–$45)** — a digital product. A guide, a workbook, a template. Low risk, high value, scalable.
- **Core ($100–$500)** — your signature offer. A course, a small group, a focused service.
- **Premium ($1,000+)** — high-touch. 1:1 work, a mastermind, done-for-you.

Most women try to start at Premium because it sounds like the goal, or stay stuck at Free because it feels safe. The move is to **build the Entry and Core rungs first.** They're where trust converts into income without requiring you to trade every hour for a dollar.

### Action step
Sketch your ladder. What's your Free, Entry, Core, Premium? You won't build all four now — but seeing the ladder tells you which rung to build first. (Hint: usually Entry or Core.)

---

## Lesson 4.3 — Designing the first offer

Pick one rung — most likely Entry or Core — and design it now. A real offer answers five questions crisply:

1. **Who is it for?** (Pulled straight from your calling statement.)
2. **What specific outcome does it create?** Not "growth" — a named, before/after shift.
3. **What's actually included?** The concrete deliverables. People buy clarity, not mystery.
4. **What does it cost?** A real number. (Lesson 4.4.)
5. **Why you?** The conviction and competence only you bring.

Then name it using the "The [Noun]" convention if it fits your voice — *The Study, The Reset, The Anchor.* A named offer feels like a thing, not a vague service. Things get bought.

### Action step
Run your first offer through all five questions in writing. If you can't answer #2 and #3 in plain language, the offer isn't ready — and that's useful to know now, not after launch.

---

## Lesson 4.4 — Pricing without flinching

Here's the pricing process that gets women unstuck:

1. **Start from sustainability, not comparison.** What do you need this to earn to be worth your time and to keep existing? Comparison pricing ("what does everyone else charge?") races to the bottom.
2. **Price the transformation, not the hours.** A $300 offer that saves someone two years of confusion is cheap. Stop pricing your minutes; price the gap you close.
3. **Add 20% to the number that scares you.** The number that makes you slightly queasy is usually closer to right than the one that feels comfortable. Comfortable is almost always too low.
4. **Hold it without apology.** No discount in the first sentence. No "but if that's too much…" The flinch is contagious; so is the calm.

You will feel resistance here. That resistance is the exact thing this module exists to break. Name the price. Out loud. Today.

### Action step
Set the price for your first offer using the four steps. Say it out loud to one person without apologizing or explaining. Notice the flinch — then do it anyway.

This is the asset everything else now points toward. You don't need it to be perfect. You need it to *exist* and to be priced. Done beats theoretical.
