She Initiated

The Song of Songs is not a metaphor. It is a woman's voice, her desire, and her body — taken seriously in scripture before the tradition buried it under allegory.

Seven chapters on the most radical book in the Bible: the one where a woman speaks first, acts first, and never once apologizes for any of it.

Written by MK Parrish
mkparrish.com
The Margin Notes Series
Foreword

The Book
They Couldn't
Explain Away

There is a book in the Bible that contains no mention of God. Not once. It contains no commandment, no prophecy, no doctrine, and no theology of any discernible kind. What it contains is a woman, a man, a garden, and the most explicit erotic poetry in the entire scriptural canon.

This book is the Song of Songs, also called the Song of Solomon or the Canticle of Canticles. It has been in the Hebrew Bible since before the rabbis debated its inclusion at Jamnia. It is canonical. It is authoritative. It is also the most awkward text in the tradition, because it refuses to do what the tradition has spent two thousand years telling women their bodies and desires should do: be quiet, be contained, be subordinate to someone else's authority.

The woman in the Song of Songs is not quiet. She is not subordinate. She initiates. She pursues. She speaks in the first person about what she wants and why she wants it. She goes looking for her lover in the streets at night. She opens the text — her voice is the first we hear — and she closes it. The frame of the book is her voice, her desire, her pleasure.

The tradition handled this the only way it could: by making the whole thing a metaphor. The woman is the soul. The man is God. The erotic content is really about spiritual longing. Problem solved. The most sexually explicit book in the Bible becomes a safe, symbolic document about the soul's hunger for the divine.

The problem: that's not what it is. And the tradition knew it. The allegorical reading was invented to make the book manageable. The debate about what the text actually is — and what it means that it's in the Bible at all — has never really gone away. This book is me reopening it.

If you grew up being told your body is a problem, your desire is dangerous, your sexuality is primarily a liability — the Song of Songs is not what they told you it was. It is, among other things, a corrective. One that has been in the canon the entire time.

Chapter 01

She Speaks First

The first words of the Song — and what it means that they belong to her.

Open the Song of Songs and the first voice you hear is hers.

Let him kiss me with the kisses of his mouth: for your love is better than wine. Song of Songs 1:2 (KJV)

She wants. She says so. She says it to the reader, directly, in the opening breath of the book. Not as a supplication, not as a question, not as a need to be submitted for approval. A declaration: this is what I want. This is what I'm going to pursue.

This is not incidental. The Song is structured as an alternating dialogue — her voice, his voice, sometimes the daughters of Jerusalem as a kind of chorus — but the book opens with her and closes with her. The frame is hers. The story is hers.

The Hebrew makes her agency even clearer than the translation suggests. The verbal forms she uses are active and indicative. She is not asking permission. She is not being given desire by a man. She is announcing a state of affairs and, shortly, acting on it. This is a woman in full possession of her own wanting.

The Grammatical Shift

Verse 2 does something grammatically interesting that most translations flatten. The woman begins by speaking about her lover in third person — "let him kiss me" — and then shifts, within the same verse, to speaking to him directly: "for your love is better than wine." He hasn't appeared yet in the text. She is summoning him, or turning toward him, in the middle of her own sentence. The movement is intimate and confident. She does not wait for him to arrive. She speaks about him and then speaks to him, collapsing the distance between absence and presence through the authority of her own voice.

She continues:

Draw me after you, let us make haste. The king has brought me into his chambers. We will exult and rejoice in you; we will extol your love more than wine; rightly do they love you. Song of Songs 1:4

Draw me after you — a command. Let us make haste — urgency. She is not passive. She is moving. She is directing. She is using imperative verbs to tell the man what she wants him to do and then, in the next breath, celebrating the fact that she's already there, in his chambers, which the text reports matter-of-factly as something she's gotten herself into.

This opening is extraordinary in the biblical context. The dominant voice in nearly every other book of the Hebrew Bible is male — male prophets, male kings, male priests, male wisdom teachers. The Song of Songs opens with a woman speaking about her body, her desire, and what she intends to do about both. She is the protagonist. She initiates. Everything else unfolds from her voice.

Why This Was Controversial

The Song of Songs was debated for inclusion in the canon at Jamnia (approximately 90 CE). Rabbi Akiba was its most famous defender, arguing that "all the writings are holy, but the Song of Songs is the Holy of Holies." His defense required reading it allegorically — as a metaphor for God's relationship with Israel. This reading enabled the book's inclusion while defusing its most confrontational element: a woman's erotic desire, expressed without shame and without masculine authorization.

The allegorical reading served a purpose. It allowed a community to keep this extraordinary text without having to fully reckon with what it says. But it also fundamentally distorted it. And the distortion has cost women something real — a canonical validation of their embodied desire that was sitting in plain sight for millennia, if only the tradition had allowed them to read it as written.

Chapter 02

The Allegory Argument

How the tradition made the Song of Songs safe — and what was lost in the process.

The allegorical reading of the Song of Songs is very old. Origin of Alexandria developed its most elaborate form in the third century CE, reading the female voice as the soul and the male voice as the Logos — the Word, Christ. Augustine and Jerome both read it allegorically. Bernard of Clairvaux wrote eighty-six sermons on the first two chapters alone, all symbolic, all spiritual, all notably careful to explain away the literal body of the woman who is speaking.

The Jewish allegorical tradition reads the book as Israel's relationship to God. The Christian allegorical tradition reads it as the soul's relationship to Christ or the Church's relationship to Christ. Both readings are theologically rich. Both have produced beautiful devotional literature. And both are exercises in making the text's most radical feature manageable.

The most radical feature is this: a woman, in the first person, describing her own erotic experience with sustained attention and evident pleasure — and the text presenting this as something to be celebrated, not corrected.

The Problem With Allegory

Allegory is a legitimate interpretive approach. The question is what it's being used to do. When allegory is applied to a text because the text genuinely suggests it — when internal markers indicate a symbolic register — allegory is illuminating. When allegory is applied because the literal meaning of the text is uncomfortable and needs to be transcended, it is avoidance wearing the costume of piety.

The Song of Songs does not suggest its own allegorical reading. There are no framing devices that signal: this is not really about what it sounds like. No narrator steps in to explain that the garden is a symbol. No character turns to the audience and says: by wine I mean grace. The text presents itself as what it is — love poetry, erotic verse, the celebration of a specific woman's body, desire, and relationship.

The allegorical reading was imported from outside. It was brought to the text by readers who needed a way to include the book without including what the book actually says about female desire. This is not interpretation. This is management.

What Happens When You Read It Literally

When you read the Song of Songs as what it appears to be — ancient erotic poetry, celebrating the sexuality and desire of a woman and a man in mutually engaged relationship — several things happen.

First: you encounter a human sexuality that is presented as good. Not dangerous, not shameful, not in need of careful caveating and containment. Good. The bodies described are beautiful. The desire described is celebrated. The garden setting echoes Eden, but here there is no fall. There is no shame. There is only two people, their bodies, and the world responding to them in bloom.

Second: you encounter a female sexuality that is centered. The woman's pleasure is not incidental. It is not background to the man's desire. She has her own experience, her own language for it, her own knowledge of what she wants and why. The man's beauty and body are described through her eyes. She is the one who frames the experience, who pursues it, who speaks about what she finds.

Third: you encounter a theology of embodiment that has been largely absent from Christian tradition. The body is not a problem to be transcended. It is not the seat of dangerous desire that needs to be subdued by spirit. It is the site of an encounter that is, in this text, entirely sacred — sacred not by metaphor but by its own nature. Two humans in physical and emotional relationship, in a garden, surrounded by beauty and fragrance and the natural world. This is not fallen experience. This is fully human experience, presented without apology.

The Song of Songs does not need to be about God to be sacred. It might be saying that human love, in its fullness, already is.

Rabbi Akiba Was Right, Just Not How He Meant It

The Song of Songs is the Holy of Holies. Rabbi Akiba was right about that. But its holiness does not come from its allegorical content. It comes from its willingness to say — in the middle of a canon that is often deeply anxious about women's bodies and desires — that this, too, is holy. That her wanting is not a danger. That her body is not a problem. That she gets to speak, in the first person, about her own experience, and the text frames her as right to do so.

That is a radical theological statement. It deserves to be heard as what it is.

Chapter 03

Her Body In
Her Own Words

The wasfs — the physical descriptions — and what it means that she describes his body before he describes hers.

The Song of Songs contains extended physical descriptions of both the man and the woman. In Hebrew poetry, these are called wasfs — a term borrowed from the Arabic literary tradition — and they are a set piece of ancient Near Eastern love poetry. They describe the beloved's body part by part, using imagery from nature, architecture, animals, and landscape.

Here is a section of the woman describing her lover:

My beloved is radiant and ruddy, distinguished among ten thousand. His head is the finest gold; his locks are wavy, black as a raven. His eyes are like doves beside streams of water, bathed in milk, sitting beside a full pool. His cheeks are like beds of spices, mounds of sweet-smelling herbs. His lips are lilies, dripping liquid myrrh. His arms are rods of gold, set with jewels. His body is polished ivory, bedecked with sapphires. His legs are alabaster columns, set on bases of gold. His appearance is like Lebanon, choice as the cedars. His mouth is most sweet, and he is altogether desirable. This is my beloved and this is my friend, O daughters of Jerusalem. Song of Songs 5:10-16

She is looking at him. Specifically, attentively, with pleasure. She knows his body. She describes it from head to legs, moving through the parts of him she has evidently studied. The description is frank, physical, and entirely without apology. She does not qualify her looking. She does not ask permission to see him this way. She simply says: this is what I see. This is what I have been looking at. This is my beloved.

The Sequence Matters

Scholars have noted that in the Song's structure, the woman describes the man's body first. The man's extended description of the woman's body (4:1-7 and 7:1-9) comes later in the text. The woman's gaze precedes the man's. She is not the one being looked at, assessed, evaluated — waiting for his verdict on her appearance. She is the one looking, and the text gives her that authority first.

In nearly every other context in the ancient world — and in most contemporary visual culture — the dynamic runs the other way. The woman's body is the object of the male gaze. She exists to be seen by him. His desire creates her desirability; his looking defines her worth. The Song of Songs inverts this. She looks first. Her looking is authoritative and pleasurable. His body exists, in this text, partly for her to see.

This is not a small literary choice. It encodes a vision of mutual desire in which neither party is purely object and neither is purely subject. Both are seen. Both are seeing. The Song does not present desire as something that flows from man to woman, requiring her to receive and respond. It presents desire as something both parties have, both parties express, and both parties enact.

When He Describes Her

The man's description of the woman is equally extended and similarly attentive:

How beautiful you are, my love, how very beautiful! Your eyes are doves behind your veil. Your hair is like a flock of goats, moving down the slopes of Gilead. Your teeth are like a flock of shorn ewes that have come up from the washing, all of which bear twins, and not one among them has lost its young. Your lips are like a crimson thread, and your mouth is lovely. Your cheeks are like halves of a pomegranate behind your veil. Song of Songs 4:1-3

He sees her. He names what he sees. The imagery is abundant and specific — natural, agricultural, architectural. These are not generic compliments. They are the observations of someone who has looked with sustained attention. And she receives his looking without anxiety. She does not shrink. She does not redirect. When he speaks, she does not disappear into his narrative of her.

The mutuality is the point. The Song is a text about two people in genuine relationship — not a transaction, not a hierarchy, not a construction in which one party exists for the other's satisfaction. Both parties want. Both parties are wanted. Both bodies are present and worthy of description. The text treats this as the natural order of things, not as an exception requiring explanation.

Chapter 04

The Night She
Went Looking

Song of Songs 3 and 5 — two scenes that have made the tradition deeply uncomfortable, and why they should be read exactly as written.

Twice in the Song of Songs, the woman leaves her house at night to go looking for her lover. These scenes are among the most unusual in the biblical canon — and among the most revealing about what the text is actually doing.

Upon my bed at night I sought him whom my soul loves; I sought him, but found him not; I called him, but he gave no answer. "I will rise now and go about the city, in the streets and in the squares; I will seek him whom my soul loves." I sought him but found him not. The sentinels found me, as they went about in the city. "Have you seen him whom my soul loves?" Scarcely had I passed them when I found him whom my soul loves. I held him, and would not let him go until I brought him into my mother's house, and into the chamber of her who conceived me. Song of Songs 3:1-4

She wakes at night, restless. She goes into the city alone. She interrogates the night watchmen — the sentinels, the guardians of urban order — asking them about her lover. She finds him and brings him back to her mother's house. The action belongs entirely to her. She initiates. She searches. She finds. She holds and brings him home. The man in this passage is almost entirely passive — present only as the object of her pursuit.

The Second Night Scene

The parallel scene in chapter 5 is darker and more complex:

I slept, but my heart was awake. Listen! my beloved is knocking. "Open to me, my sister, my love, my dove, my perfect one; for my head is wet with dew, my locks with the drops of the night." I had put off my garment; how could I put it on again? I had bathed my feet; how could I soil them again? My beloved thrust his hand into the opening, and my inmost being yearned for him. I arose to open to my beloved, and my hands dripped with myrrh, my fingers with liquid myrrh, upon the handles of the bolt. I opened to my beloved, but my beloved had turned and was gone. Song of Songs 5:2-6

She goes out after him again. And this time, the night watch finds her differently:

Making their rounds in the city, the sentinels found me; they beat me, they wounded me, they took away my mantle, those sentinels of the walls. Song of Songs 5:7

This is the verse the tradition has almost never known what to do with. The guardians of the city beat a woman and take her clothing — and the text reports this as part of her experience, embedded in her own first-person account, without editorializing it away. She has been assaulted. She continues her search anyway. The next verse has her adjuring the daughters of Jerusalem to help her find him.

What This Scene Is Doing

Several things are happening here simultaneously, and all of them are important.

First: the text does not blame her for going out. It does not frame the assault as punishment for her transgression, as retribution for a woman who violated the social rules about female movement. It presents the violence as something that happened to her — unjust, painful, experienced — without suggesting she caused it by her own agency.

Second: her response to the violence is not to retreat. She continues. She is wounded and still asking for help locating her beloved. Her desire is not extinguished by the assault. Her determination to pursue what she wants is not broken by the fact that the city's guardians tried to break it.

Third: the scene is realistic about the world women navigate. Ancient women moving through cities at night faced real danger. The Song does not pretend otherwise. But it tells this woman's story from the inside of her experience — not from the perspective of the authorities who beat her, not from the perspective of a narrator who would have her stay home. From her perspective, in her words, her search continues.

This is not comfort literature. This is honest literature. And honest literature about women's experience — in which the woman's desire, her agency, and her perspective remain central even when the world tries to stop her — is rarer than it should be, including in scripture.

She was wounded and kept going. The text does not tell her she was wrong to want what she wanted. Neither should we.

Chapter 05

Dark
and Lovely

The Shulammite's self-description — and the long history of misreading it as shame.

The woman of the Song describes herself near the opening of the book, and the description has been misread for so long that most people have entirely the wrong impression of it.

I am black and beautiful, O daughters of Jerusalem, like the tents of Kedar, like the curtains of Solomon. Do not gaze at me because I am dark, because the sun has gazed on me. Song of Songs 1:5-6a (NRSV)

The King James Version — the translation that shaped English-speaking Christianity for four hundred years — renders this: "I am black, but comely." That conjunction has done significant damage. Black but beautiful — as if the two were in tension. As if her darkness were a condition to overcome on the way to beauty. As if she were acknowledging a deficiency and immediately qualifying it.

The Hebrew conjunction is waw, which simply means and. I am black and beautiful. There is no adversative in the original. No concession. No "but." Two descriptions, coordinate, of equal weight: dark and lovely. The "but" in the KJV is not a translation. It is an interpretation — one that smuggled a self-deprecating register into a statement that, in the Hebrew, carries none.

Who She Is

The woman of the Song is identified as the Shulammite — a term whose exact meaning is uncertain, possibly indicating she is from a place called Shulem or Shunam, or possibly a feminine form of Solomon, creating a kind of paired title for the two lovers. She is also clearly a woman who works outdoors: her brothers made her keep the vineyards, she says, and the sun has tanned her skin. She is not an aristocrat. She is a working woman, and her body shows it.

Her relationship with her brothers is notably tense. They made her work in the vineyards, she says — the implication being against her preference or for their benefit. She adds: "my own vineyard I have not kept." This is often read metaphorically — the vineyard as herself, as her own flourishing, as the things she would tend if she had the choice — but it may also be straightforwardly literal. She worked for her brothers. She had less autonomy than she wanted over her own time and body.

And yet she is not defined by her brothers' authority or by the limitations of her circumstances. She is defined, in the text, by her own voice — by what she wants, what she sees, how she describes herself and the world around her. Her skin is sun-dark and she says so without apology. She is beautiful and she says so without qualification.

Race, Color, and the History of This Text

The woman's description of her dark skin has been a site of extraordinary interpretive abuse. For centuries of Western Christian commentary, her darkness was read as spiritual darkness — a metaphor for sinfulness, for gentile identity, for spiritual need. The association of dark skin with spiritual deficiency was not incidental to Western racial theology; texts like this one were used to support it.

It is necessary to say clearly: the woman of the Song of Songs describes her skin as dark and describes herself as beautiful in the same breath. The text does not treat her darkness as a problem. The tradition that read it as spiritual deficiency was importing its own racial anxieties into a text that simply — beautifully, directly — says: I am dark. I am beautiful. These are not contradictions. They are both true.

Whatever else the Song says, it says this: a dark-skinned woman's self-assessment is that she is beautiful, and the text does not contradict her. In a world — then and now — that has consistently told dark-skinned women that their beauty is conditional or requires qualification, that is not a small thing to find in a canonical text. It is something to read closely, and to read without the "but."

Chapter 06

What the Daughters
of Jerusalem Know

The chorus, the community, and the vision of female solidarity the Song quietly builds.

The Song of Songs is not a solo. It is a dialogue, and embedded within it is a third voice — or rather, a third category of voice: the daughters of Jerusalem.

The daughters of Jerusalem appear throughout the poem as a kind of intimate audience, a female community to whom the Shulammite speaks, asks questions, and occasionally issues commands. They are her listeners. They adjure her. She adjures them. They ask her questions about her lover and she answers. They function as something between chorus, friends, and witnesses.

I adjure you, O daughters of Jerusalem, by the gazelles or the wild does: do not stir up or awaken love until it is ready! Song of Songs 2:7

This refrain — "do not stir up or awaken love until it is ready" — appears three times in the Song. It is the Shulammite speaking, and she is speaking to other women. She is sharing something hard-won: that desire has its own timing, that love moves in its own season, that there is a readiness that cannot be forced. She is not asking for advice. She is giving it.

A Community of Women

The existence of the daughters of Jerusalem is quietly significant. The Song does not present the woman's experience as isolated or singular. She has a community. She has people she can speak to about what she wants, who she wants, what her lover looks like, what she's feeling. When she describes his body in chapter 5, she is describing it to them — the daughters of Jerusalem ask her what makes her beloved so special, and she answers with the extended physical description we've already looked at.

This is women talking to each other about their lives, their bodies, their desires. The text does not present this conversation as scandalous or inappropriate. It presents it as ordinary — the kind of conversation that happens between women who know each other well enough to ask and answer honestly.

The tradition's anxiety about female sexuality has often manifested as anxiety about women talking to each other about their sexuality. Women who share information, experience, and knowledge about their own bodies and desires are, in many contexts, treated as either morally suspect or dangerously subversive. The Song of Songs presents women doing exactly this — speaking to each other about desire, about the body, about what love feels like and what it demands — and frames it as part of the natural landscape of the poem. The daughters of Jerusalem are just there. Speaking. Listening. Being female community for each other.

The Question They Ask

What is your beloved more than another beloved, O fairest among women? What is your beloved more than another beloved, that you thus adjure us? Song of Songs 5:9

The daughters of Jerusalem ask the Shulammite: what makes him so special? It is a question that invites the Shulammite to articulate her experience — to name what she sees, what she knows, why she is the way she is about this person. Their asking creates space for her to speak. Her speaking is the content of chapter 5's extended description of his body.

This is, in its quiet way, a model of something: women creating space for each other to name their experience. The question is neither suspicious nor prurient. It is curious, warm, inviting. And the answer it elicits is the Shulammite at her most precise and beautiful.

The daughters of Jerusalem are not minor characters. They are the community that holds the Shulammite's story. They are the ones who hear it. And the Song would not be the Song without them.

Chapter 07

The Body As
Sacred Text

What the Song of Songs, read honestly, tells us about what the Bible actually believes about embodiment, desire, and the goodness of physical life.

There is a theology of the body latent in the Song of Songs that the tradition has largely failed to articulate because it was too busy making the book safe. Let me try to articulate it.

The Song of Songs presents the body as a site of legitimate, celebrated, sacred experience. Not sacred because it represents something else. Not sacred because we can read it allegorically and find God hiding in it. Sacred on its own terms — because two human beings in genuine relationship, fully present in their bodies, experiencing desire and beauty and connection, are doing something that the text treats as worthy of extended, careful, beautiful attention.

The natural world in the Song is not incidental backdrop. It is participant. The garden blooms because the lovers are in it. The seasons mark their reunion and separation. The fragrances — myrrh, nard, henna — attach to their bodies and their presence. The landscape is not a metaphor for their relationship. Their relationship is part of the landscape, woven into the natural world as something equally real and worthy of celebration.

My beloved speaks and says to me: "Arise, my love, my fair one, and come away; for now the winter is past, the rain is over and gone. The flowers appear on the earth; the time of singing has come, and the voice of the turtledove is heard in our land." Song of Songs 2:10-12

This is, among other things, a poem about the intersection of human love and the natural world. Spring arrives and the man calls to the woman — or vice versa, the correspondence is mutual — and the blooming of the world and the blooming of the relationship are inextricable. The body is not separate from the earth. The desire of the body is not separate from the rhythms of the created world. The Song places human sexuality in the context of creation and treats both as good.

Correcting the Tradition's Body Theology

Western Christianity inherited from Greek philosophy a deep suspicion of the body. Plato divided reality into the material (inferior, unstable, mortal) and the ideal (eternal, perfect, real). The body belonged to the material. The soul belonged to the ideal. The project of the philosophical life — and, by extension, for many early Christians, the spiritual life — was to discipline, transcend, or escape the body's claims.

This body theology was not the only theological option available in the ancient world. The Hebrew tradition, from which Christianity emerged, was substantially more positive about embodied existence. Creation in the Hebrew Bible is called good — repeatedly, definitively, including the human body. The Psalms celebrate the human form. The prophets speak of God's relationship to Israel in bodily terms. The incarnation — God becoming human, taking on a body — is, in Christian theology, the most definitive statement of God's affirmation of embodied existence.

And yet the body-suspicion of Greek philosophy, filtered through Tertullian and Augustine and Jerome, became the dominant Christian attitude toward the body and sexuality. Women's bodies in particular became sites of danger — the seat of temptation, the gateway for evil, the problem that needed to be managed by male authority and clerical oversight.

The Song of Songs was always sitting there, in the canon, saying the opposite. The body is not a problem. Desire is not dangerous. A woman looking at a man, describing what she sees, pursuing what she wants — this is presented as an occasion for poetry, not for reprimand.

The most body-affirming text in the Bible is the one where a woman speaks in the first person about her own desire. That is not a coincidence.

What Reclaiming This Means

If you grew up being told your body was a problem — too much, too tempting, too present, too in need of covering and controlling and apologizing for — the Song of Songs has something specific to say to you.

Your body is not the problem. The tradition that made it the problem was wrong — exegetically wrong, as we've seen, drawing on philosophical assumptions that the text itself does not support. The Bible's most sustained treatment of a woman's physical experience presents it as worthy of literary celebration. The woman's desire is the engine of the poem. Her body is described, by herself and by her lover, as beautiful. Her pleasure is not incidental. It is the point.

Reclaiming the Song of Songs as what it actually is — not an allegory, not a spiritual metaphor, but a canonical celebration of embodied, mutual, female-centered erotic experience — is not a radical or revisionist move. It is a return to the text. It is reading the book that is actually in the Bible, instead of the safe version that was invented to replace it.

The woman of the Song knew who she was and what she wanted. She said so, in the first words of the book. She never stopped saying so. Two thousand years of allegorical reading tried to make that voice into something else. It didn't work. The voice is still there. It was always there. You just have to read it as written.

Closing Note

The Song of Songs is available in every translation. I recommend reading it in at least two: the NRSV for accuracy, and the translation by Ariel and Chana Bloch (The Song of Songs: A New Translation) for poetry. The Bloch translation preserves the Hebrew rhythms and images better than almost any other English version and reads the book without allegorical overlay. Phyllis Trible's treatment of the Song in God and the Rhetoric of Sexuality remains the best scholarly feminist reading I've encountered. Ellen Davis's chapter on the Song in Getting Involved with God is short, careful, and genuinely moving. For the tradition of allegorical reading — if you want to understand what was done to this text — Bernard of Clairvaux's On the Song of Songs is the primary source, and it is remarkable both for its beauty and for how completely it transforms the poem into something it is not.
