Foreword

Before We
Begin

I want to tell you something before we get into any of this. I am not a theologian. I did not go to seminary. I have never stood at a pulpit or held a doctorate in biblical studies.

What I am is a woman who read the text. Carefully. With a concordance, a stack of scholarly translations, and the specific kind of fury that comes from realizing you have been lied to — not through malice, necessarily, but through centuries of accumulated interpretation handed down by people who had every reason to read it the way they did.

I grew up in church. I know the story you were told. Eve sinned first, so women are more susceptible to deception. Eve sinned first, so women must be silent. Eve sinned first, so submission is the cost of the fall. Eve sinned first, and we have been paying for it ever since.

I also know how exhausting it is to love something — a text, a tradition, a community — while also knowing that the version of it you've been handed is incomplete. Maybe wrong. Definitely shaped by people who were not writing with you in mind.

This book is not an attack on the Bible. I want to be clear about that. What it is: a close reading. An honest one. The kind that asks what the text actually says before it asks what the tradition says the text means. Those are different questions. They have been collapsed into one for so long that most people don't realize they're two.

You don't need to hold any particular theological position to read this book. You don't need to believe the Bible is inerrant. You don't need to have left the church. You don't even need to be sure you believe in God. What you need is the willingness to look at a story that has shaped the lives of billions of women and ask: did we actually read this, or did we read what someone else told us it said?

That's a question worth asking. This is me asking it out loud.

Let's go.

Chapter 01

The Garden
Before The Fall

What you were never told about who was created, when, and what it means.

Most people who grew up in church were taught the creation story as a sequence. God made the world. God made man from the dust. God saw that man was alone and made woman from his rib. Then woman ruined everything.

It's a tidy narrative. It's also, when you go back and read what Genesis actually says, a significant oversimplification — and in several places, a distortion.

Let's start at the beginning. Genesis 1 and Genesis 2 are two different accounts of creation. Biblical scholars — including conservative ones — have recognized this for centuries. They differ in sequence, style, emphasis, and in what they tell us about the nature of the humans being created. The first account is sweeping, cosmic, poetic. The second is intimate, focused, domestic. They are not contradictory. But they are not the same story told twice, and treating them as identical has created a number of the problems we'll be untangling in this book.

So God created humankind in his image, in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them. Genesis 1:27 (NRSV)

Notice what Genesis 1 says: God created humankind — ha-adam in Hebrew — in the divine image. Male and female, simultaneously, together, in the image of God. There is no secondary creation here. There is no delay. The first account of creation describes the simultaneous existence of gendered humanity, both bearing the image of God with equal clarity and equal force.

The word ha-adam in Genesis 1 is not a proper name. It is a collective noun — a word for humankind, or earth-creature, derived from adamah, meaning earth or ground. It becomes a personal name later. At this point in the text, it refers to the species. To all of us. To the collective human creature made in the image of the divine.

Already, before we've even reached chapter two, the narrative that woman was an afterthought — a secondary creation, derivative, less than — is contradicted by the text itself.

The Second Account

Genesis 2 tells the story differently. Here, God forms a single human creature from the dust of the ground — ha-adam again — breathes life into it, and places it in the garden. This creature is, at this point in the text, undifferentiated. The Hebrew does not say man. It says earth-creature. The differentiation into male and female comes later, in the moment of woman's creation.

This is not a small thing. It means the story in Genesis 2 is not: man exists, then woman is added. It is: undifferentiated human creature exists, then from that creature, two sexed beings emerge. Woman's creation is not the addition of a secondary character. It is the completion of the story. The differentiation into male and female is what makes Adam, Adam — and what makes the woman, the woman. Neither exists fully as gendered until both exist.

She is not his supplement. She is his completion — and simultaneously, her own.

The rib. Everyone knows the rib story. God takes a rib from the man and builds the woman from it, and this has been used for centuries to suggest that woman is, by design, secondary. Made from man. Made for man. Derivative.

The Hebrew word translated as "rib" is tsela. It appears forty-one times in the Hebrew Bible. In every other instance — every single one — it is translated not as rib but as side. As in: the side of the ark. The side of the tabernacle. The side of the building. It is an architectural term, and it is consistently translated as such everywhere except this single passage, where it becomes "rib" — a choice that renders the construction of woman far more diminutive than the text requires.

Read it again with the correct translation: God took a side — a full side, a substantial half — from the earth-creature and built from it a woman. The earth-creature becomes two. One side becomes Adam-male. The other side becomes woman.

Does that sound like an afterthought to you?

What the Garden Was

Before we get to the fall, we need to establish what the garden was — and who was in charge of it.

Genesis 2 is very clear. God places the earth-creature in the garden with a mandate: to tend and to keep it. These are active, authoritative verbs. The Hebrew is abad (to work, to serve) and shamar (to guard, to keep, to watch over). These are not passive caretaking words. They are the same words used elsewhere in the Hebrew Bible for the priestly service of the sanctuary.

The garden was a sacred space. The human creature was its priest.

When woman is created and brought to the man, she enters this same space with the same mandate. Nothing in the text restricts her authority or her access. She moves freely. She speaks. She reasons. When the serpent approaches her, he addresses her not because she is gullible or inferior — but because, as the one who speaks, engages, and acts in the narrative, she is the active agent in the garden at that moment.

Which brings us to the question everyone has an opinion about: the fruit, the serpent, and what actually happened there.

A note on translations

Throughout this book, I'll draw from multiple translations — the NRSV, the ESV, the NIV, the CEB, and the original Hebrew. Translations are interpretations. The choice of a single English word can encode centuries of theological assumption. Wherever translation choice matters to the argument, I'll flag it.

Chapter 02

Ezer
Kenegdo

The word mistranslated for two thousand years — and what it actually means for every woman who has been told she exists to support someone else's story.

There is a phrase in Genesis 2:18 that has done more damage to the spiritual imagination of women than almost anything else in the text. In most English translations, it goes like this: "It is not good for the man to be alone; I will make him a helper suitable for him."

Helper. That's the word. Helper — as if woman were an assistant. A support staff. A background character in someone else's leading role. Women have been handed this word for millennia as both a description and a prescription: you are made to help. Your purpose is auxiliary. Your identity is relational, and that relation is fundamentally subordinate.

The problem is that this is not what the Hebrew says. Not even close.

The Hebrew Word Is Ezer

The word translated "helper" in English is ezer. It appears twenty-one times in the Hebrew Bible. Let's look at how it's used everywhere else — because context is everything, and the context here is damning.

Sixteen of those twenty-one uses refer to God. Specifically, to God as a source of strength, rescue, and power when Israel is in desperate need.

Our help (ezer) is in the name of the LORD, who made heaven and earth. Psalm 124:8
I lift up my eyes to the hills — from where will my help (ezer) come? My help (ezer) comes from the LORD, who made heaven and earth. Psalm 121:1-2
O Israel, trust in the LORD! He is their help (ezer) and their shield. Psalm 115:9

The remaining uses — the five that don't refer directly to God — refer to military allies. Nations that come to Israel's aid in times of crisis. Reinforcements. Strength from outside that turns the tide.

Not a single use of ezer in the entire Hebrew Bible carries a connotation of subordination, inferiority, or auxiliary function. Every use of this word points toward power. Strength. Rescue. The kind of help that saves a situation — not the kind that files paperwork.

When God says "I will make for the man an ezer," the word being used is the same word used for God's own aid to Israel. The strength of God himself, made tangible and embodied. That is what the text calls her.

The Second Word: Kenegdo

The phrase is ezer kenegdo. We've looked at ezer. Now let's look at kenegdo.

Most translations render it as "suitable for him," or "corresponding to him," or "fit for him." The Hebrew kenegdo is built from the word neged, which means in front of, opposite, counterpart. The suffix -o means "him." So literally: in front of him. Opposite him. His counterpart.

This is not the language of subordination. It is the language of correspondence, of mirroring, of facing. Two things of equal standing, positioned across from each other. An opponent in a debate. A partner in a dance. A counterpart, not a complement in the diminishing sense of the word.

The full phrase ezer kenegdo is better translated: a strength that is his equal, standing opposite him. Or: a power that corresponds to his own, facing him.

Not made from him in the sense of being lesser. Made from him in the sense of being the other half of something that could not exist as whole without both sides present.

Now go back and read Genesis 2:18 with those words intact: "It is not good for the man to be alone; I will make for him a power equal to his own, facing him as his counterpart."

Different, isn't it?

Why This Translation Matters

You might be thinking: fine, the word is stronger than "helper." But does it really change that much?

It changes everything.

If woman is created as ezer — as the same kind of strength the psalmists reach for when they need God's rescue — then her fundamental design is not auxiliary. Her fundamental design is power. Strength. The capacity to intervene decisively in a situation that cannot sustain itself without her.

This reframes every place in the tradition where women have been told to make themselves smaller, quieter, less. It reframes every theology of female submission that grounds itself in creation order — because if the creation order tells us anything, it tells us that woman was made to be a force, not a footnote.

The distortion of ezer into "helper" is not a neutral translation choice. It is a theological statement. One that was made by men in a particular cultural moment, encoded into the English language, and handed to generations of women as the voice of God.

It is not the voice of God. It is the voice of a translator. Those are not the same thing.

Helper Is Not a Dirty Word

I want to be careful here, because I'm not arguing that helping others is degrading. It's not. Care, support, presence — these are among the most powerful things any human being can offer another.

What I'm arguing is that the specific use of "helper" to describe the ontological function of woman — what she was made for, at her core, before anything else — is both exegetically wrong and practically harmful. It takes the strongest word in the Hebrew Bible for the kind of power that rescues, saves, and turns the tide, and renders it as something quieter, softer, and supportive.

When a woman has been told her entire life that she was made to help — to subordinate her own initiative, vision, and authority to someone else's leadership — and she reads the text for herself and finds the word is ezer, something breaks open in her. I've seen it happen. I've felt it myself.

The text was not written against you. That part came later.

Chapter 03

He Was Standing
Right There

The verse they never preached on — and what it means that Adam said nothing while the serpent spoke.

Let's talk about the moment everyone knows. The serpent. The fruit. The woman who listened. The fall of mankind, laid at her feet for the last two thousand years.

Here's the verse you grew up with:

Now the serpent was more crafty than any other wild animal that the LORD God had made. He said to the woman, "Did God say, 'You shall not eat from any tree in the garden'?" Genesis 3:1 (NRSV)

The serpent speaks to the woman. She engages. She reasons — actually, she reasons quite carefully, citing the exact command she was given and the consequence attached to it. She is deceived by the serpent's counter-argument and eats the fruit. Then she gives some to her husband. He eats.

Standard interpretation: woman is deceived first, therefore more susceptible to deception, therefore should be silent and subordinate. This is not a fringe reading. This is the mainstream interpretation that has governed church policy on women for most of Christian history. Paul invokes it directly in 1 Timothy 2.

Now let me show you the verse they always skipped.

So when the woman saw that the tree was good for food, and that it was a delight to the eyes, and that the tree was to be desired to make one wise, she took of its fruit and ate; and she also gave some to her husband, who was with her, and he ate. Genesis 3:6 (NRSV)

Four words. Who was with her.

Adam Was There

The husband who eats the fruit was with her. Right there. Present for the entire conversation. The serpent spoke. The woman reasoned. The exchange happened. And standing beside her, the entire time, was Adam — silent, passive, present, and about to make his own choice.

He doesn't speak. He doesn't intervene. He doesn't remind her of the command. He doesn't say: wait, this doesn't seem right. He stands there and then, when offered the fruit, he eats it.

No deception. No dialogue. No resistance. Just a hand extended, a fruit accepted, and a man making a silent, complicit choice while the woman he supposedly leads does all the moral reasoning.

And yet the story we've been told is: she deceived him. She led him astray. She was the initiating agent of the fall. He was a passive victim of her feminine wiles.

The text doesn't say any of that. Genesis 3:6 places him at the scene, present and silent, making his own uncomplicated decision to eat.

He wasn't tricked. He wasn't deceived. He was there, and he chose. That matters.

Who Gets Called Out First

After the eating, things unravel. God comes looking. The humans hide. And then comes the most revealing sequence in the entire chapter: the interrogation.

But the LORD God called to the man and said to him, "Where are you?" Genesis 3:9

God calls for Adam. Adam explains that he was afraid. God asks: who told you you were naked? Have you eaten from the tree I commanded you not to eat from?

God does not address the woman first. God holds the man accountable. This is consistent with the structure of the passage: the command about the tree was given to the man in Genesis 2, before the woman was created. It was his responsibility. And God goes to him first.

Adam's response is extraordinary in its cowardice:

The man said, "The woman whom you gave to be with me, she gave me fruit from the tree, and I ate." Genesis 3:12

He blames her. Explicitly. And notice the construction: not just "the woman," but "the woman whom you gave to be with me" — implying that God is also at fault for giving him such a woman. He's deflecting responsibility to two people in a single sentence. The woman made me do it. And you gave her to me, so really, this is on you.

This is the man who has been held up as the moral and spiritual standard for male headship. The one who, in the critical moment of the only rule that existed in the garden, was silent, compliant, and then, when caught, immediately redirected blame onto his wife and his God.

The Problem of "Eve Sinned First"

Let's deal with this directly, because it's the cornerstone of the theological argument for female subordination.

Paul writes in 1 Timothy 2:14: "Adam was not deceived, but the woman was deceived and became a transgressor." This is often read as a statement of Eve's special culpability — she sinned first, she was deceived, women are therefore more susceptible to spiritual deception and must be guarded by male authority.

But read it carefully. Paul is distinguishing between Adam's transgression and Eve's. Eve was deceived — she acted under false information, genuinely believing the serpent's reframing. Adam was not deceived — he knew what he was doing. He ate anyway. Willfully. Without the excuse of deception.

If we're assigning moral culpability, the person who sins knowingly is more culpable than the person who sins while deceived. That's basic moral philosophy. And yet the tradition has flipped this on its head, using Eve's deception as an indictment and Adam's willful transgression as a footnote.

The argument that women must be silent and subordinate because Eve was deceived first also proves too much. If we're going to make creation and fall narratives into permanent, universal prescriptions for behavior, then Adam's explicit willful disobedience should generate at least as many restrictions on men. It doesn't. The argument is applied selectively in one direction, which tells us something important: this is not exegesis. This is a social structure being dressed up as theology.

What the Text Actually Blames

Romans 5 — the clearest New Testament reflection on the fall — does not blame Eve. Paul writes: "Just as sin came into the world through one man, and death came through sin..." One man. Adam. Specifically, deliberately, Adam. The entire theological architecture of original sin in Christian tradition runs through Adam, not Eve. When the tradition wants to establish the fall as a structural, cosmic event, it rightly identifies Adam as the agent. When the tradition wants to restrict women, it pivots to Eve. You cannot have it both ways.

The story of the garden is not a story about women's weakness. It's a story about the first human choice — made in full knowledge, in partnership, with consequences for everyone. The woman reasoned. The man followed. Neither came out of it clean. The asymmetry in how that story has been told and applied is not the Bible's asymmetry. It's ours.

Chapter 04

The Curse
Is Not a Command

Descriptive versus prescriptive — the distinction that changes everything about what you've been told to accept.

After the garden scene, God speaks to each party in turn: the serpent, the woman, and the man. What God says to the woman is the verse that has been used to argue that female submission is both God's design and God's command.

To the woman he said, "I will greatly increase your pangs in childbearing; in pain you shall bring forth children, yet your desire shall be for your husband, and he shall rule over you." Genesis 3:16 (NRSV)

He shall rule over you. There it is. The verse. The one that shows up in sermons on submission, in books on biblical womanhood, in arguments against women in leadership. God himself said it: the husband rules. This is the created order, restored after the fall, and women must accept it.

There is a grammatical problem with this reading, and it is significant enough to restructure the entire argument.

What God Is Doing Here

Look at the structure of Genesis 3. God speaks to the serpent, the woman, and the man. In each case, God describes what the world will now look like as a consequence of what happened. The serpent will crawl on its belly and eat dust. The woman will experience pain in childbirth and a distorted relational dynamic with her husband. The man will struggle against the earth that was supposed to cooperate with his work; it will produce thorns and thistles and he will earn his food by the sweat of his brow.

Notice what God does not say to the man: "You shall sweat." Notice what God does not say to the serpent: "You shall crawl." The text uses indicative language — language that describes future reality — not imperative language. These are predictions of consequence, not divine commands.

The distinction between descriptive and prescriptive is one of the most basic moves in biblical interpretation. A prescriptive text tells you what to do. A descriptive text tells you what is or will be. "You shall not murder" is prescriptive — a command. "You will experience pain in childbirth" is descriptive — a prediction. The fall curses are not commandments. They are descriptions of the broken world that results from the choice that was made.

God describing what a fallen world looks like is not the same as God endorsing it, commanding it, or asking you to maintain it.

The Anesthetic Problem

Here is a test for the prescriptive reading that most people have never thought about.

If Genesis 3:16 — "in pain you shall bring forth children" — is a divine command, then the use of anesthesia during childbirth is a violation of God's design. Women are supposed to experience that pain. It is part of the created order, decreed by God as both consequence and command.

When anesthesia was first introduced in the 1840s, this argument was actually made. Seriously. By theologians and clergy. The pain of childbirth was God's ordained will, and to circumvent it was to rebel against the divine order.

We find this argument absurd today. No modern theologian argues that epidurals are sinful. No church preaches that women should refuse pain management during labor because Genesis 3 commands it. We universally accept that the pain of childbirth is a consequence of the fall — a brokenness we rightly try to mitigate — not a divine instruction to be obeyed.

But those same churches, in those same sermons, turn to Genesis 3:16b — "he shall rule over you" — and call it a command. They cannot have it both ways. Either the whole verse is prescriptive, including the pain of childbirth, or the whole verse is descriptive, describing what a broken world looks like. Pick one. Apply it consistently.

The inconsistency reveals that what's happening here is not careful exegesis. It is selection. The parts of the curse that are inconvenient or medically unnecessary are treated as descriptive. The part that enforces a particular social hierarchy is treated as prescriptive. That is not how you read a text honestly. That is how you use a text to justify a conclusion you've already reached.

The Word For "Desire"

There's a second exegetical argument that needs addressing here, because it comes up in nearly every complementarian treatment of this passage.

The Hebrew word translated "desire" in Genesis 3:16 — "your desire shall be for your husband" — is teshuqah. It appears three times in the entire Hebrew Bible. In Genesis 3:16, in Genesis 4:7, and in Song of Songs 7:10.

In Genesis 4:7, God uses it when warning Cain: "sin is lurking at the door; its desire is for you, but you must master it." Here, teshuqah refers to sin's drive to control, to overtake, to dominate. Many complementarian theologians — most notably Susan Foh in a 1975 paper that became enormously influential — argued that the "desire" in Genesis 3:16 should be read the same way: woman's desire to usurp, to control, to overpower the man, which must be resisted by male rule.

The problem: in Song of Songs 7:10, the same word appears: "I am my beloved's, and his desire is for me." Here it is clearly and straightforwardly erotic desire — not a drive to dominate, but a turning toward, a longing for. No one argues that the beloved is trying to overpower her lover in Song of Songs.

The word can mean several things. The meaning chosen for Genesis 3:16 — sinister desire for domination — was chosen by a theologian with a prior commitment to female subordination, not determined by the word itself. And it became the foundation of an enormously consequential theological argument.

A more straightforward reading of Genesis 3:16 in its totality: your longing will be toward your husband, and he will dominate you. It is a description of the relational distortion that enters the world through the fall. An asymmetry that was not present in the garden before the choice was made. A description of what goes wrong — not a prescription for how things should be.

If the Curse Is Not a Command

If Genesis 3:16 is descriptive — a diagnosis of what breaks in human relationship when the relationship with God breaks — then the project of the gospel is not to enforce it. It is to undo it.

This is exactly what the rest of the New Testament suggests is happening. Galatians 3:28: "There is no longer Jew or Greek, there is no longer slave or free, there is no longer male and female; for all of you are one in Christ Jesus." This verse has been limited, qualified, and explained away by people who need it to be about spiritual equality only — heavenly status, not earthly function. But the paired hierarchies it dismantles — Jew/Greek, slave/free — are also earthly social structures. There is no consistent reason to read the third pair, male/female, as spiritual-only.

If the redemptive project of the New Testament is the undoing of the curse — if thorns and thistles are to be worked against, if pain in childbirth is to be alleviated, if all of the brokenness the fall introduced is something Christians are called to push back against rather than accept — then the domination of women by men is not something to enforce. It is something to heal.

You were not designed for subjugation. The text that has been used to tell you otherwise is being misread. That is not a small thing. That is the thing.

Chapter 05

The Men Who
Wrote The Rules

Augustine, Tertullian, Jerome, and the specific, traceable moment when women's spiritual inferiority became official church doctrine.

It is a common assumption that the subordination of women is simply what the Bible teaches — ancient, self-evident, outside the reach of historical analysis. This assumption is wrong. The theology of female inferiority has a history. It has authors. We know their names.

The most significant of them: Tertullian, writing in the late second and early third century; Augustine of Hippo, writing in the late fourth and early fifth century; and Jerome, translator of the Latin Vulgate and prolific letter-writer, whose views on women shaped Catholic theology for a thousand years. These are not minor figures. They are the architects of Western Christian thought. And their views on women were extreme, specific, and rooted not primarily in the biblical text but in the philosophical assumptions of their cultural moment.

Tertullian

Tertullian is responsible for one of the most damaging sentences in the history of Christian writing. Addressing women directly in his treatise On the Apparel of Women, he wrote:

You are the devil's gateway. You are the unsealer of that forbidden tree. You are the first deserter of the divine law. You are she who persuaded him whom the devil was not valiant enough to attack. You destroyed so easily God's image, man. On account of your desert — that is, death — even the Son of God had to die.

This is not metaphor. This is not rhetorical excess. Tertullian is making a theological claim: every woman, by virtue of being a woman, shares in Eve's guilt. Every woman is a gateway for the devil. Every woman is responsible for the death of Christ. This claim has no exegetical basis. The Hebrew text does not say it. The New Testament does not say it. Paul's letters — complex and contested as they are on gender — do not say it. Tertullian said it, and it became the bedrock of patristic theology on women.

Tertullian also wrote: "Woman is a temple built over a sewer." He argued that women's nature was inherently more connected to the body, to sexuality, to the material world — and therefore further from the divine. He did not arrive at these conclusions from scripture. He arrived at them from Neoplatonic dualism, the Greek philosophical tradition that divided reality into mind (good) and matter (bad), and associated women with the latter.

Augustine

Augustine is more nuanced than Tertullian, which is part of what makes him more dangerous. He was a brilliant theologian and a genuinely complex thinker. He is also responsible for the doctrine that shaped Western Christian anthropology for fifteen hundred years: that woman is not fully made in the image of God.

Augustine's argument, made across several of his works, goes like this: man, in himself, fully images God. Woman, in herself, does not. Woman images God only in relation to man — only when she is considered as part of the man-and-woman pair, not as an individual. He grounds this in his reading of 1 Corinthians 11:7 ("he is the image and glory of God; but woman is the glory of man") and imports it into his reading of Genesis.

Let's be direct: this reading contradicts Genesis 1:27, which says, plainly, that God created humankind — male and female — in the image of God. Both. The image of God is not distributed unevenly between them in the text. Augustine's counter-reading requires significant exegetical gymnastics and was contested by theologians even in his own time.

But Augustine won. He always does. His theological weight is such that his conclusions became the mainstream of Western Christian thought — Catholic and, later, Protestant. The idea that woman has a diminished or derivative relationship to the image of God filtered through centuries of church teaching and created the theological ground for her exclusion from leadership, authority, and full spiritual personhood.

Jerome

Jerome translated the Hebrew Bible into Latin in the late fourth century. The Vulgate was the Bible for Western Christianity for a thousand years. When Jerome made translation choices, they were not academic choices. They were choices that shaped what every educated person in Europe understood the Bible to say.

Jerome's attitude toward women was, to put it charitably, complicated. He admired certain women, corresponded with them, educated them, and genuinely valued their intellect. He also wrote: "Woman is the root of all evil." He idealized female celibacy and expressed contempt for female sexuality. His translations reflect these biases — subtle, sometimes, but present. The choice to render ezer as "adiutorium" (helper, assistant) rather than something closer to its military or divine-aid meaning was Jerome's. It was not inevitable. It was a choice.

Why This History Matters

This history matters because the theology of female subordination is not as old as Christianity. It is not the natural and inevitable reading of the text. It is the reading of specific men, in specific cultural contexts, carrying specific philosophical assumptions about gender, matter, and the body that they inherited from Greek philosophy and then read backward into the Hebrew text.

The patristic theologians were brilliant. They were also products of their world — a world that assumed female inferiority as a fact of nature. When they read the Bible through that assumption, they found what they were looking for. That's how confirmation bias works. That's how it has always worked.

The question for us is not whether we can respect their intellect. We can. The question is whether we continue to uncritically receive their conclusions as the voice of God when we have access to the original text, to two thousand years of counter-argument, and to the straightforward experience of women whose spiritual lives, gifts, and authority are not diminished by their gender.

The Church Fathers were not the last word. They were men writing in a moment. Read the text yourself. You have that right.

Chapter 06

Paul's Letters
Are Not Universal Policy

The two silence passages — what they actually say, to whom, and why context isn't optional when you're reading someone else's mail.

The New Testament has two primary texts used to restrict women in church: 1 Corinthians 14:34-35 and 1 Timothy 2:11-15. These are the "silence passages." They have been wielded for centuries as proof that women must not speak or teach in Christian contexts. Let's read them — carefully, with context, the way you're supposed to read any letter.

First principle: Paul's letters are letters. They are not systematic theological treatises. They are pastoral correspondence — written to specific people, in specific communities, to address specific problems. Reading a letter without knowing the situation it addresses is like reading someone's reply to an argument you weren't present for. You have half the conversation. The conclusions you draw will be incomplete at best, wildly wrong at worst.

1 Corinthians 14: The Corinth Problem

Women should be silent in the churches. For they are not permitted to speak, but should be subordinate, as the law also says. If there is anything they desire to know, let them ask their husbands at home. For it is shameful for a woman to speak in church. 1 Corinthians 14:34-35 (NRSV)

Here is what you need to know about Corinth: it was a mess. The Corinthian church was chaotic. Speaking in tongues was happening without interpretation, to the point of incomprehensibility. People were getting drunk at communion. There was a sexual scandal Paul had to address. There were factions, lawsuits between members, disputes about food sacrificed to idols. And there were — almost certainly — women who were disrupting the assembly.

This matters because Paul is not writing a universal prohibition. He is addressing a specific disorder in a specific church. The "silence" passages in 1 Corinthians 14 are embedded in a passage about order in worship — about doing things decently and in sequence, about not having everyone speak at once, about the prophets taking turns. It is not a passage about the nature of womanhood. It is a passage about chaos.

More importantly: three chapters earlier, in 1 Corinthians 11, Paul gives instructions on how women should pray and prophesy in the assembly. Pray and prophesy. That is not silence. If Paul meant for women to be categorically silent in every church for all time, he would not have spent verses in chapter 11 regulating the way they speak. The contradiction exists in plain text. Something is being addressed situationally, not categorically.

Additionally: many textual scholars — including some of the most rigorous conservatives — have noted that 1 Corinthians 14:34-35 appears in different locations in different early manuscripts. In some manuscripts, these verses appear after verse 40 rather than after verse 33. This kind of variation suggests to many scholars that these verses may be a later scribal insertion — what's called a gloss — that crept into the text from a margin note. If true, these two verses are not Paul's words at all. This is not a fringe position. It is held by scholars at evangelical institutions.

1 Timothy 2: The Ephesus Problem

Let a woman learn in silence with full submission. I permit no woman to teach or to have authority over a man; she is to keep silent. 1 Timothy 2:11-12 (NRSV)

1 Timothy is one of the Pastoral Epistles — letters to Timothy and Titus about how to order the churches under their care. These letters address specific problems in specific locations. Timothy was in Ephesus. Ephesus was a city with a large, influential cult centered on the goddess Artemis, one of whose primary sites was there. It was also a city where, by the early second century, Gnostic and proto-Gnostic teachings were spreading rapidly — teachings that included alternative creation myths in which Eve was the enlightened one, the first teacher, the spiritual superior to the deceived Adam.

Paul is almost certainly writing into this context. The prohibition on women teaching is most coherently read as a response to women who are teaching false doctrine — specifically, doctrine rooted in the Artemis cult or in early Gnostic reinterpretations of the Genesis narrative. "I do not permit a woman to teach or to exercise authority over a man" is Paul addressing a specific local crisis, not issuing a universal decree.

The word translated "authority" in 1 Timothy 2:12 is also worth examining. The Greek word is authentein. It appears exactly once in the New Testament. Nowhere else. In the Greek literature of the period, authentein often carries negative connotations — domineering, usurping, controlling. It is not the ordinary Greek word for authority (exousia), which Paul uses freely in other places. The choice of this specific, unusual, negatively-tinged word suggests that what Paul is prohibiting is not legitimate female teaching authority but a specific kind of inappropriate, domineering behavior — one that was actively occurring in Ephesus.

The Women Paul Commended

Whatever Paul meant in these two passages — and scholars have been arguing about it for centuries — we have to set it against the full picture of how Paul actually treated women in his ministry. Because the full picture is significantly different from the selective quotation.

In Romans 16, Paul commends Phoebe as a diakonos — the same word translated "deacon" when applied to male church leaders — and asks the Roman church to receive her and help her, because she has been a leader (prostatis) to many, including Paul himself. He greets Prisca (Priscilla) before her husband Aquila — a reversal of the expected social order that is almost certainly intentional. He calls Junia "outstanding among the apostles" — a woman, named as an apostle, outstanding among them.

He writes in Galatians 3:28 that in Christ there is no male and female. He addresses women who prophecy. He works alongside women as ministry partners.

This is not the record of a man who believed women had no spiritual authority or voice. This is the record of a man writing situational guidance to specific communities with specific problems, guidance that has been extracted from its context and applied universally in ways that contradict his own practice.

What We Do With This

I'm not asking you to dismiss Paul. I'm asking you to read him as what he is: a first-century apostle writing urgent pastoral letters to real communities dealing with real chaos, using language and arguments shaped by his moment, addressing situations we must reconstruct from incomplete evidence.

The letters in the New Testament are not a law code. They are not a policy manual. They are correspondence — brilliant, complex, contextual. They require interpretation, not just quotation. And when we read them alongside what Paul actually did with real women in real ministry, the picture that emerges is substantially more complex than "Paul said women should be silent."

Two verses, removed from context, have been used to silence half the church for two thousand years. That is worth looking at carefully. Not dismissively. Carefully. With the text open. With the history in hand. With the honesty to say: maybe we've been reading someone else's mail wrong.

Chapter 07

The Women Who
Showed Up Anyway

Deborah. Mary Magdalene. Priscilla. Phoebe. Junia. The women the text celebrates, and what they were doing while the tradition was building its case against them.

If the Bible actually teaches the comprehensive spiritual subordination of women, it is remarkable how many women in the Bible seem not to have received the memo.

The biblical text is full of women who lead, judge, prophesy, teach, fund, organize, and initiate — women who are not peripheral figures but who are central to the story the Bible is telling. The tradition has dealt with these women in various ways: by minimizing them, by explaining away their authority as exceptional circumstances rather than normative models, by treating them as interesting footnotes rather than part of the sustained argument the text is making. Let's look at a few of them directly.

Deborah: Judge and Prophet

Deborah is one of the judges of Israel. The judge was not a ceremonial role. The judge was the supreme civil and military leader of the nation — the highest political and juridical authority. Deborah held this role. She judged disputes. She received the word of God and delivered it. She commanded military campaigns. She is introduced in Judges 4 without apology, explanation, or qualification. The text does not say "although she was a woman" or "because there was no man available." It says she was a prophet. It says she was judging Israel. It describes her authority as a matter of established fact.

At that time Deborah, a prophet, wife of Lappidoth, was judging Israel. She used to sit under the palm of Deborah between Ramah and Bethel in the hill country of Ephraim; and the Israelites came up to her for judgment. Judges 4:4-5

This is not a woman operating in a support role. This is not a woman functioning under male authority. This is the chief leader of the nation of Israel, exercising prophetic and judicial authority over men and women alike, during a period that the book of Judges presents as Israel's founding era.

If comprehensive female subordination were a fixed biblical principle, Deborah would not exist in the text. She does. She is celebrated. The song in Judges 5 that commemorates her victory is among the oldest poems in the Hebrew Bible, and it exults in her leadership without reservation.

Mary Magdalene: Apostle to the Apostles

Mary Magdalene has been one of the most systematically misread figures in Christian history. Pope Gregory I, in a 591 sermon, conflated her with the unnamed sinful woman in Luke 7 and with Mary of Bethany, creating the composite "fallen woman" figure who repented and followed Jesus. This conflation has no textual basis. The Catholic Church officially repudiated it in 1969, issuing a formal correction. The "reformed prostitute" story has been theologically retracted by the institution that created it. Most people still believe it.

What the text actually tells us: Mary Magdalene was a disciple of Jesus — named first in every list of female disciples, which suggests she was the most prominent among them. She was present at the crucifixion. She was present at the burial. And she was the first witness to the resurrection, the first person to whom the risen Christ appeared, and the one commissioned to go and tell the apostles what had happened.

Jesus said to her, "Mary!" She turned and said to him in Hebrew, "Rabbouni!" (which means Teacher). Jesus said to her, "Do not hold on to me, because I have not yet ascended to the Father. But go to my brothers and say to them..." John 20:16-17

The resurrection witness is the foundation of the Christian faith. "If Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile," Paul writes. The witness to the resurrection is the most important testimony in Christian history. And Jesus chose a woman to deliver it. Not to the women who would believe her easily. To the apostles. To the male inner circle who would go on to lead the church. She was sent to them with the news they would found everything on.

The early church fathers called her "apostle to the apostles" — apostola apostolorum. She was sent. She bore witness. She proclaimed. She was, in the most precise technical sense, an apostle. The tradition spent centuries obscuring this with the prostitute myth. The myth has been retracted. The text remains.

Priscilla: Teacher of Apollos

In Acts 18, a man named Apollos arrives in Ephesus. He is described as eloquent, well-versed in scripture, fervent in spirit, and accurate in what he knows about Jesus — but his knowledge of the faith is incomplete. He knows only the baptism of John. Priscilla and her husband Aquila hear him and take him aside. They explain the way of God to him more accurately.

This is teaching. Priscilla is teaching a man — an eloquent, scripture-trained man — a more complete understanding of the gospel. She is not supporting her husband's teaching. She is named first; most scholars read this as a marker of her primacy in the teaching relationship. She is listed before her husband in four of the six times the couple is mentioned in the New Testament — a consistent reversal of the expected social order that almost certainly indicates she was the more prominent figure.

If women are prohibited from teaching men, Priscilla is in violation. But the text presents her teaching as a commendable act, not a cautionary tale. Apollos goes on to be one of the most important figures in the Corinthian church. Priscilla helped make him who he was.

Phoebe and Junia

We've touched on both of these women, but they deserve a direct mention. Phoebe is called a diakonos — deacon — of the church at Cenchreae and a prostatis of many, including Paul. Some translations soften prostatis to "benefactor" or "helper," but the word has a primary meaning of leader, patron, one who stands before others with authority and resource. Paul asks the Roman church to assist her in "whatever she may require from you" — the language of providing support to a leader on mission.

Junia is called "outstanding among the apostles" in Romans 16:7. For most of Christian history, commentators assumed this was a man named Junias. There is no evidence in Greek literature of a male name "Junias." Junia is a common Roman female name. The male reading was an assumption made because people couldn't accept that a woman had been called outstanding among the apostles. John Chrysostom, the fourth-century bishop of Constantinople, wrote of Junia: "How great the wisdom of this woman that she was even deemed worthy of the title apostle." He had no trouble reading the name as female. Later interpreters did.

What These Women Tell Us

The Bible does not portray women as spiritually inferior or inherently subordinate. It portrays women who lead, who judge, who prophesy, who teach, who fund, who witness, who are sent. It portrays women at the center of the most significant events in both testaments. It names them, commends them, and uses their authority as models.

The tradition that built a comprehensive theology of female subordination did so by reading certain texts selectively, ignoring others, and filling gaps with philosophical assumptions it had imported from Greek thought. The women of the Bible were not minor characters. They were practitioners of a faith that was, before the tradition hardened around it, significantly more generous toward female authority than what most of us inherited.

You are in good company. The women who went before you showed up anyway. So can you.

Chapter 08

The Reread

What you do with all of this — and what it means to take the text seriously enough to read it honestly.

We've been through a lot. Let me summarize what we've actually established, because I want you to be able to hold it clearly before we talk about what to do with it.

  • Genesis 1 describes the simultaneous creation of male and female in the image of God, without hierarchy or sequence.
  • Genesis 2 uses the word ezer kenegdo for woman — a word meaning strength equal to his own, the same word used for God's own aid to Israel — not "helper" in the auxiliary sense.
  • Adam was present and silent during the entire garden scene, ate the fruit willingly without deception, and then blamed his wife and God when confronted.
  • The fall curse in Genesis 3:16 is descriptive, not prescriptive — a diagnosis of what breaks, not a command to preserve the brokenness.
  • The theology of female spiritual inferiority was constructed by specific men — Tertullian, Augustine, Jerome — who imported Greek philosophical assumptions into their biblical reading.
  • Paul's silence passages are situational correspondence, not universal policy, and contradict his own practice of working with and commending women in ministry.
  • The Bible is full of women who lead, judge, prophesy, teach, and witness — and who are commended for it.

This is not a radical reading. These arguments have been made by careful, rigorous scholars for centuries — including scholars who hold high views of biblical authority and do not want to undermine the text. They are mainstream in biblical studies. They are mainstream in a growing number of Christian communities. What has often kept them from reaching women in the pews is that the people preaching from those pulpits had every institutional reason to keep a reading in place that consolidated their own authority.

What This Is Not

This is not an argument that the Bible is wrong. I want to be precise about this, because the dismissive counter-argument is always: you just don't like what the Bible says, so you're reinterpreting it.

I do not think the Bible says women are spiritually inferior, inherently subordinate, or categorically prohibited from teaching and leading. I think the text has been interpreted through a lens it didn't arrive with, by people with interests in reading it a particular way, and that the interpretation became so entrenched it felt like the text itself. What I'm arguing is not that we should override the Bible. I'm arguing that we should read it — actually read it, in the original language, with its historical context, with attention to the full range of its testimony on women.

When you do that, the picture looks different. Not because the text has changed. Because you're reading it now, instead of reading the accumulated interpretation of two thousand years of mostly male commentary.

What To Do With Your Church

I can't answer this for you, and I won't try. The question of whether to stay, to push back, to find a different community, or to find a different relationship with the whole thing entirely is yours. It depends on what your church means to you, what it costs you to stay, what it costs you to leave, and what your life can actually sustain.

What I will say is this: you are allowed to ask questions. You are allowed to read the text yourself. You are allowed to bring scholarship to the table. You are allowed to say: I don't think this reading is right, and here's why. You are allowed to hold your own authority in relation to scripture — not because your authority is greater than the text, but because your access to the text is as direct as anyone else's.

The woman who reads the Bible in her kitchen at 11pm and catches something the pastor missed is doing exegesis. She may not have the credentials. She has the text. Those are not the same thing, but they are also not as different as institutions would like them to be.

What To Do With Your Faith

If what you've been handed has been used to silence you, diminish you, make you doubt your own perceptions and authority — I'm not going to pretend that a careful rereading of Genesis fixes that. Theological harm is real harm. It lodges in the body. It shapes how you move through the world, how much space you take up, whether you trust your own voice.

What the rereading can do is give you something solid to stand on. If the text itself doesn't say what you've been told it says — if the subordination wasn't written into the creation, wasn't decreed in the fall, wasn't the consistent teaching of the apostles — then you aren't fighting the Bible when you insist on your own full humanity. You're reading it correctly.

That matters more than I can say. Not because doctrinal accuracy is the point, but because so many women have internalized the idea that their spiritual instincts are dangerous, their authority is illegitimate, their leadership is at best tolerated and at worst sinful. That internalization was put there by a reading. Another reading can begin to take it out.

You were made as strength equal to his own, facing him as his counterpart. The text has always said so. You just weren't shown that part.

Eve

Let's go back to the beginning. To Eve. To the woman at the center of the story that has been used against women for two millennia.

Here is what I see when I read the Genesis text without the weight of the tradition pressing down on it:

I see a woman who was created from the side of another creature, bringing full differentiation and completion to both. I see a woman who was named strength equal to his own. I see a woman who moved freely in a sacred space. I see a woman who engaged a difficult conversation while the man beside her was silent. I see a woman who reasoned, who was deceived, who made a choice — a choice her husband also made, without the excuse of deception. I see a woman who was pronounced as a mother of all living, who appears in genealogies, who is never treated in the Hebrew text as the origin of all evil.

I see a woman who got blamed for everything — by men who needed an origin story for their own dominance, who found one in a selective reading of a text that doesn't actually give it to them.

Eve was not the problem. She was the original ezer: a strength that faced the most difficult moment in the garden and engaged it head-on. She was wrong about what the serpent told her. She was not wrong about who she was.

Neither are you.

For Further Reading

The arguments in this book draw on decades of rigorous biblical scholarship. If you want to go deeper, these are the scholars and texts I recommend most:

  • Phyllis Trible — God and the Rhetoric of Sexuality. The foundational feminist close reading of Genesis 2-3. Rigorous, beautiful, essential.
  • Bruce Waltke — Genesis: A Commentary. A conservative scholar who nevertheless acknowledges the ezer kenegdo translation problem. Useful for readers who need the argument made from within the tradition.
  • Lucy Peppiatt — Rediscovering Scripture's Vision for Women. The best accessible treatment of the 1 Corinthians and 1 Timothy passages I've encountered.
  • N.T. Wright — Surprised by Hope and his essays on Junia and women in the New Testament. Mainstream evangelical scholarship that takes female apostolic authority seriously.
  • Scot McKnight — The Blue Parakeet: Rethinking How You Read the Bible. A good introduction to the question of why we apply some biblical texts universally and others situationally.
  • Lynn Cohick and Amy Brown Hughes — Christian Women in the Patristic World. The history of women's actual roles in early Christianity — messy, contested, and significantly more expansive than the tradition admits.
