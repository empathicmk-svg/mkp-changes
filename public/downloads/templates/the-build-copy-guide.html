What this is

Building a website is not primarily a design problem. It's a copy problem. Most websites fail at the sentence level — the headline doesn't say anything, the about page talks only about the company, the services page lists features with no outcomes. A beautiful site with weak copy is a beautiful building with no address.

What's included

Section 1: Before You Write Anything

The positioning brief

One page. Five questions. Every word in the site comes from the answers to these five questions:

  1. Who is this for? (One specific person, not a category.)
  2. What's the one thing we do better or differently than anyone else?
  3. What does the reader most need to believe in order to hire us?
  4. What's the single most common objection — and what's the honest response to it?
  5. What do we want them to do right now?

If you can't answer all five, the copy isn't the problem yet.

The voice snapshot

Three adjectives, three banned words, one "sounds like us" sentence. Your voice snapshot prevents your website from reading like it was written by a committee of people who've never met.

Section 2: The Homepage

The homepage has one job: convince the right person they're in the right place and direct them to one next step.

The hero section

  • H1 framework: [What you do] for [who] — stated with a verb, not a noun. "We build financial models for early-stage founders" beats "Financial Strategy Solutions."
  • Subhead framework: The specific outcome your best clients get, in one sentence.
  • CTA copy: Three options for the button — when to use "Get Started," "Book a Call," "See Our Work," and why "Learn More" converts nothing.

The social proof strip

Three types of proof in order of effectiveness: specific outcomes (revenue, saves time, solves what), named client quotes (with company + title), and recognizable logos. Frameworks for writing each.

The services preview

The three-card rule: no more than three service cards on the homepage. For each card: one-line title, one-line outcome, one CTA. Templates and examples.

The about preview

One paragraph, written about what you believe — not who you are. The about preview on the homepage is not a bio. It's a conviction statement. Template and examples.

The final CTA section

The offer, restated simply, with the friction-reducing phrase that addresses the last objection before the click. Templates by service type.

Section 3: The Services Pages

A services page fails in one of two ways: it lists features with no outcomes, or it's so outcome-focused it forgets to describe what the person actually gets.

The services page framework (per service):

  • Header: Service name + one-line outcome statement
  • The problem section: Three to four sentences that describe the situation this service addresses. Written as their problem, not your pitch.
  • The solution section: What you do and what it produces — in concrete terms. "You get X, Y, and Z. Within [timeframe], you'll have [deliverable]."
  • The process section (optional): Three to five steps. Makes the abstract feel real.
  • The proof section: One case study or quote, specific to this service.
  • The CTA: Price anchor (or "starting from") + booking link.

Templates for six types of service pages: retainer, project, digital product, course, coaching, and done-for-you.

Section 4: The About Page

The most miswritten page on almost every website. Most About pages are about the company. The best About pages are about the reader — their problem, told through the lens of how you came to care about it.

The About page framework:

  1. The empathy opener: Start with them, not you. One paragraph that names the situation they're in.
  2. The bridge: How you came to understand that situation. The true version.
  3. The conviction: What you believe that shapes how you do the work. Not a values list — a point of view.
  4. The credentials: Brief, specific, and ordered by relevance not prestige.
  5. The human detail: One non-professional detail. People buy from people.
  6. The CTA: One next step.

Templates and a full annotated example.

Section 5: The Contact & Booking Pages

The last page people visit before they decide. Most contact pages abandon the visitor at the worst moment.

  • The micro-headline: One line that justifies the friction of the form. Examples by service type.
  • The expectation-setter: What happens after they submit. Specific timeline. No one likes not knowing.
  • Form field guidance: Which fields to include, which to cut (every extra field reduces completions), and how to phrase questions so they don't feel like interrogation.
  • The booking page: Pre-booking language that warms the conversation before it starts. What to say above a Calendly embed.

Section 6: The One Test

Every sentence in your site, run through one test: Does this sentence help the right person take the right action, or does it make us feel good?

Copy that makes the company feel good: "We are a leading provider of integrated strategic solutions." Copy that helps the right person: "We write the investor pitch decks that get to the next meeting."

The test is not elegant. It's just useful. Run every sentence through it before the site goes live.
